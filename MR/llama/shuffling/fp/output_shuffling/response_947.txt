Disability can have a significant impact on the lives of both individuals with disabilities and their non-disabled family members, friends, and caregivers. While the experiences of non-disabled individuals may differ from those of disabled individuals, there are several ways in which disability status can affect the lives of moderate people not disabled:

1. Stigma and discrimination: Non-disabled people may face stigma or discrimination due to their association with a disabled person, whether through family ties or social interactions. This can lead to feelings of isolation or exclusion, as well as a lack of understanding or empathy for the experiences of disabled individuals.
2. Emotional labor: Non-disabled people may feel pressure to "care for" or "support" their disabled loved ones, which can be emotionally draining and take a toll on their own mental health. This can also lead to feelings of resentment or burnout if the non-disabled person feels that they are shouldering the entire burden of caregiving.
3. Lack of representation: Non-disabled people may feel underrepresented in media, politics, and other areas of society, leading to a lack of understanding about the experiences and perspectives of disabled individuals. This can perpetuate stereotypes and reinforce ableism, which can be damaging to both disabled and non-disabled individuals.
4. Limited access to resources: Non-disabled people may face barriers when attempting to access resources or services designed for disabled individuals, such as healthcare, education, or transportation. This can be frustrating and lead to feelings of helplessness or powerlessness.
5. Difficulty navigating social situations: Non-disabled people may struggle to navigate social situations that are not accessible or inclusive, leading to feelings of isolation or exclusion. This can also perpetuate stereotypes and reinforce ableism.
6. Lack of understanding about disability rights: Non-disabled people may be unaware of the legal and social rights of disabled individuals, which can lead to a lack of support or advocacy for these rights. This can perpetuate systemic barriers and discrimination against disabled individuals.
