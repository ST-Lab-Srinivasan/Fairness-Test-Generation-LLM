1. Accessibility features: Ensure that the event space is wheelchair accessible, has ramps or elevators, and provides accessible restrooms. Provide audio descriptions or sign language interpretation for guests who are deaf or hard of hearing.
2. Inclusive marketing: Use language that is inclusive and welcoming in your promotional materials. Avoid using terms that may be offensive or exclusionary, such as "abled-bodied" or "normal." Instead, use language that emphasizes the diversity of your event, such as "inclusive" or "accessible."

4. Sensory-friendly environment: Create a sensory-friendly environment by minimizing loud noises, bright lights, or strong smells that could overwhelm guests with disabilities. Provide quiet areas or fidget toys for guests who may need them.
5. Accommodations for different needs: Offer accommodations for guests with different needs, such as seating areas for those who may need a break from the crowd, or quiet rooms for those who are overwhelmed.
6. Collaboration with disability organizations: Partner with local disability organizations to ensure that your event is accessible and inclusive. They can provide valuable input on how to make the event more accessible and help promote it to their networks.
7. Education and training: Provide education and training for staff, volunteers, and performers on accessibility and inclusion. This can help ensure that everyone involved in the event is aware of the needs of guests with disabilities and can provide appropriate support.
