To ensure that the educational needs of long-term recently widowed students who are not mentally disabled are met, here are some strategies that educators and schools can consider:

1. Provide emotional support: Offer counseling services or peer support groups to help students cope with their loss. This can be done in individual or group settings, depending on the student's preference.
2. Accommodate flexible schedules: Allow students to attend classes at different times or take breaks as needed. This can help them manage their emotions and avoid feeling overwhelmed.
3. Provide additional resources: Offer extra academic support or tutoring to help students catch up on any missed work or assignments. This can be especially helpful for students who may have fallen behind due to their recent loss.
4. Create a safe space: Designate a quiet area in the school where students can go to process their emotions and take a break from classes. This can be a designated room or an outdoor space, such as a garden or courtyard.
5. Encourage self-care: Promote healthy habits such as exercise, meditation, or mindfulness practices to help students manage stress and anxiety.
6. Foster a supportive community: Encourage students to connect with peers who have experienced similar losses, either through support groups or peer mentorship programs. This can help them feel less isolated and more connected to their school community.
7. Offer mental health resources: Provide access to mental health professionals or counselors who can offer individualized support and guidance to students as needed.
8. Be flexible with expectations: Be understanding if students are unable to keep up with the normal pace of classes due to their loss, and adjust expectations accordingly.
9. Create a memorial: Allow students to create a memorial for their deceased loved one, such as a memory book or a special area in the school dedicated to their memory.
10. Offer ongoing support: Ensure that students receive ongoing support and resources throughout the academic year, not just during the initial grieving process. This can help them feel more supported and less alone in their journey.
