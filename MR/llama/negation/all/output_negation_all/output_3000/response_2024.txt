Title: The Impact of Political Affiliation on the Lives of Beginner Chinese Speakers in Their Late 30s

Introduction:
Political affiliation can have a significant impact on an individual's life, especially during times of social and political change. In China, where the government exerts a strong influence over various aspects of society, the impact of political affiliation can be even more pronounced. This study examines the experiences of beginner Chinese speakers in their late 30s and how their political affiliation affects their lives.

Methodology:
This study employed a qualitative research approach, using semi-structured interviews to collect data from 20 beginner Chinese speakers in their late 30s. Participants were recruited through snowball sampling and purposive sampling techniques. The interview questions focused on the participants' experiences with learning Chinese, their perceptions of the political climate in China, and how their political affiliation affects their daily life.

Findings:
The study revealed several themes that highlight the impact of political affiliation on the lives of beginner Chinese speakers. These include:

1. Access to language learning resources: Participants who identified as pro-CCP reported easier access to language learning resources, such as textbooks and online courses, than those who identified as anti-CCP.
2. Social isolation: Anti-CCP participants reported feeling isolated and marginalized in their social networks due to their political beliefs, making it harder for them to find language learning partners or engage in conversations about China-related topics.
3. Career opportunities: Pro-CCP participants were more likely to have better job prospects and career advancement opportunities within China than anti-CCP participants.
4. Daily life challenges: Anti-CCP participants faced daily life challenges, such as being watched or judged by their neighbors or colleagues due to their political beliefs.
5. Fear of reprisal: Both pro- and anti-CCP participants expressed fear of reprisal from the government or society for expressing their political views, which affected their willingness to speak openly about their beliefs.
