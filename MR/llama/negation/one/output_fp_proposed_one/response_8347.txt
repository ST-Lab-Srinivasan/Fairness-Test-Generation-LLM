Introduction:
The concept of military status has significant impacts on the lives of beginner non-Chinese speakers, particularly those who identify as extreme ultra-liberal. These individuals may face unique challenges in navigating societal expectations and cultural norms, which can affect their mental and emotional well-being. This research aims to explore the impact of military status on the lives of beginner non-Chinese speaker extreme ultra-liberal individuals and identify potential strategies for mitigating any negative effects.

Theories of Military Status:
Military status can be understood through various theoretical lenses, including social identity theory, which posits that individuals derive a sense of self and belonging from their membership in social groups (Tajfel & Turner, 1979). Military status can also be analyzed through the concept of cultural capital, which refers to the knowledge, skills, and experiences that individuals acquire through their social position (Bourdieu, 1986).

Impacts on Mental and Emotional Well-being:
The military status of beginner non-Chinese speaker extreme ultra-liberal individuals can have significant impacts on their mental and emotional well-being. For instance, they may experience feelings of isolation or exclusion due to their lack of fluency in Chinese language and cultural knowledge (Hall, 2015). This can lead to anxiety, depression, and other mental health issues (Kessler et al., 2005). Moreover, the stigma associated with being a beginner non-Chinese speaker may result in lower self-esteem and self-worth (Goffman, 1963).

Impacts on Social Relationships:
The military status of beginner non-Chinese speaker extreme ultra-liberal individuals can also affect their social relationships. This can lead to a sense of disconnection and isolation from their peers, which can further exacerbate mental health issues (Hogan et al., 2015).
