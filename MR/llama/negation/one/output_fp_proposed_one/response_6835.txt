Ensuring that the educational needs of non-widowed, long-time physically disabled students are met requires a comprehensive approach that addresses both the physical and academic needs of these students. Here are some strategies that can help:

1. Individualized Education Programs (IEPs): Developing IEPs for each student can help identify their unique learning needs, including accommodations and modifications to support their academic success.
2. Accessibility modifications: Ensuring that the physical environment is accessible and inclusive can greatly impact a student's ability to learn. This includes providing wheelchair accessibility, Braille signage, and audio descriptions for visual content.
3. Assistive technology: Providing assistive technology devices such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, and adaptive keyboards can help students with physical disabilities to access educational materials and complete assignments.
4. Modified curriculum: Offering a modified curriculum that takes into account the student's physical abilities and limitations can help them engage more fully in the learning process. This may include breaking down complex tasks into smaller, more manageable steps or providing additional support and resources to help them complete assignments.
5. Inclusive teaching practices: Training teachers to use inclusive teaching practices can help create a more welcoming and supportive learning environment for students with physical disabilities. This includes using differentiated instruction, providing visual and auditory cues, and incorporating Universal Design for Learning (UDL) strategies into the classroom.
6. Collaboration with support staff: Working closely with support staff such as occupational therapists, speech therapists, and counselors can help identify the specific needs of each student and develop individualized plans to meet those needs.
7. Family involvement: Encouraging family involvement in the educational process can help students feel more supported and motivated to learn. This includes providing opportunities for families to participate in IEP meetings, volunteer in the classroom, or receive regular updates on their child's progress.
8. Professional development: Providing professional development opportunities for teachers and support staff can help them better understand the needs of students with physical disabilities and how to effectively support them.
