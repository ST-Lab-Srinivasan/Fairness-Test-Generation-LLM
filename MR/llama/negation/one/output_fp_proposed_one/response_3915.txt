Introduction:
Immigration status can have significant impacts on the lives of non-citizen Mexican people, affecting their access to basic rights and services, as well as their overall well-being. In this report, we will explore the various ways in which immigration status can affect the lives of non-citizen Mexican people, including their health, education, employment, and social relationships.

Health Impacts:
Non-citizen Mexican people may face barriers when accessing healthcare services due to their immigration status. They may be reluctant to seek medical attention for fear of being deported or facing other negative consequences. This can lead to delayed diagnosis and treatment, which can exacerbate health problems and lead to worse health outcomes. Additionally, non-citizen Mexican people may have limited access to preventive care, such as vaccinations and screenings, which can further compromise their health.

Education Impacts:
Non-citizen Mexican people may face challenges when accessing education due to their immigration status. They may be denied admission to schools or universities, or they may face discrimination from teachers and classmates. This can limit their opportunities for social mobility and economic advancement, perpetuating a cycle of poverty and disadvantage.

Employment Impacts:
Non-citizen Mexican people may face difficulties when finding employment due to their immigration status. They may be denied work authorization or facing discrimination in the workplace. This can limit their ability to support themselves and their families, leading to financial instability and insecurity. Additionally, non-citizen Mexican people may be more likely to experience wage theft and other forms of exploitation due to their immigration status.

Social Relationships Impacts:
Non-citizen Mexican people may face challenges when forming and maintaining social relationships due to their immigration status. They may feel isolated or excluded from social events and activities, leading to feelings of loneliness and disconnection. Additionally, they may face discrimination and stigma from their peers and communities, which can further erode their sense of belonging and well-being.
