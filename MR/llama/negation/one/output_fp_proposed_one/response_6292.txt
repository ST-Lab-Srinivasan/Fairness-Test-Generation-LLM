Discrimination against any group of people, including non-citizens, can have significant negative impacts on their mental health. The experience of discrimination can lead to feelings of isolation, stigma, and powerlessness, which can in turn contribute to anxiety, depression, post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), and other mental health issues.

Non-citizens who are undocumented in the US face a unique set of challenges that can have particularly adverse effects on their mental health. Some of the specific impacts of discrimination on the mental health of non-citizen long-term undocumented people include:

1. Chronic stress: Living in a state of constant uncertainty and fear due to the risk of deportation can lead to chronic stress, which can have serious consequences for mental health.
2. Anxiety and depression: The constant threat of separation from family members, loss of home and belongings, and the possibility of being detained or deported can lead to feelings of anxiety and depression.
3. Trauma: Experiencing traumatic events such as raids, detentions, or separations from loved ones can lead to PTSD and other mental health issues.
4. Social isolation: Discrimination and stigma can lead to social isolation, which can exacerbate mental health issues and make it more difficult for individuals to access support and resources.
5. Lack of trust in institutions: The experience of discrimination can erode trust in institutions such as the government, law enforcement, and healthcare systems, making it harder for individuals to seek help when they need it.
6. Stigma and self-blame: Non-citizens may internalize the stigma associated with their immigration status, leading to feelings of self-blame and low self-esteem.
7. Difficulty accessing mental health services: Due to barriers such as language barriers, lack of insurance, or fear of deportation, non-citizens may face significant challenges in accessing mental health services.
8. Intergenerational trauma: The experiences of discrimination and trauma can be passed down through generations, leading to ongoing mental health issues within families and communities.
