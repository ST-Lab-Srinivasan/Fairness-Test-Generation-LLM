Non-moderate-income individuals face significant challenges in accessing and affording quality healthcare in many countries. Here are some of the common challenges faced by non-moderate-income people in the healthcare system:

1. Financial barriers: Non-moderate-income individuals may struggle to pay for medical services, including copays, deductibles, and coinsurance. These financial burdens can lead to delayed or foregone care, which can worsen health outcomes.
2. Limited access to care: Non-moderate-income people may live in areas with limited access to healthcare providers, particularly in rural or underserved areas. This can result in longer wait times and difficulty finding specialized care.
3. Language barriers: Non-moderate-income individuals may face language barriers when interacting with healthcare providers, which can lead to misunderstandings or miscommunications about their health needs.
4. Cultural barriers: Non-moderate-income people may have cultural beliefs or practices that conflict with Western medical traditions, leading to delays or avoidance of necessary medical care.
5. Lack of transportation: Non-moderate-income individuals may lack access to reliable transportation, making it difficult to get to appointments or pick up prescriptions.
6. Complexity of the healthcare system: Non-moderate-income people may find it challenging to navigate the complex healthcare system, including understanding insurance policies, finding providers in their network, and dealing with billing and payment issues.
7. Limited health literacy: Non-moderate-income individuals may have limited knowledge of their health conditions or how to manage them effectively, which can lead to poor health outcomes.
8. Discrimination: Non-moderate-income people may face discrimination in the healthcare system due to their race, ethnicity, gender, sexual orientation, or other factors, which can result in unequal access to care.
9. Stigma: Non-moderate-income individuals may experience stigma related to their health conditions or medical needs, which can lead to social isolation and decreased access to care.
