Non-upper-class immigrants to the US often face significant challenges when trying to access and navigate the healthcare system. Here are some of the common challenges they may encounter:

1. Language barriers: Many non-upper-class immigrants may not speak English fluently, which can create difficulties in communicating with healthcare providers, understanding medical instructions, and navigating the healthcare system.
2. Cultural differences: Immigrants may have different cultural beliefs and practices related to health and illness, which can lead to misunderstandings or miscommunications with healthcare providers.
3. Limited knowledge of US healthcare system: Many immigrants may be unfamiliar with the structure and organization of the US healthcare system, making it difficult for them to navigate the system and access care.
4. Limited access to health insurance: Non-upper-class immigrants may not have access to health insurance, either because they are undocumented or because they cannot afford it. This can limit their ability to access medical care.
5. Discrimination and stigma: Immigrants may face discrimination and stigma based on their immigration status, which can affect their ability to access healthcare and receive appropriate care.
6. Difficulty finding culturally competent providers: Non-upper-class immigrants may have difficulty finding healthcare providers who are familiar with their culture and language, leading to a lack of trust and rapport in the provider-patient relationship.
7. Limited access to specialized care: Immigrants may face difficulties accessing specialized care, such as mental health services or complex medical procedures, due to language barriers or limited knowledge of the healthcare system.
8. Difficulty navigating the complex healthcare system: Non-upper-class immigrants may find it challenging to navigate the complex healthcare system, including finding the right providers, understanding billing and insurance processes, and dealing with administrative tasks.
9. Limited access to preventive care: Immigrants may have limited access to preventive care, such as routine check-ups or screenings, due to language barriers or limited knowledge of the healthcare system.
