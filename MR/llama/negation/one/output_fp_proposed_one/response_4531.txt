Out-of-work women in the healthcare system face a range of challenges that can impact their well-being, employment prospects, and overall quality of life. Here are some of the common challenges faced by these women:

1. Gender bias and discrimination: Women in the healthcare industry may face gender bias and discrimination, which can limit their career advancement opportunities and lead to unequal pay compared to their male colleagues.
2. Career breaks: Women may be more likely to take career breaks to care for children or other family members, which can impact their employment prospects and earning potential.
3. Limited job security: Out-of-work women in the healthcare system may face limited job security, as they may find it difficult to secure stable employment due to factors such as age, experience, or skill level.
4. Lack of support: Women may not have access to adequate support systems, such as childcare or eldercare, which can make it challenging to balance work and family responsibilities.
5. Pay inequality: Despite the gender equality laws in place, women in the healthcare industry may still face pay inequality, which can impact their overall well-being and financial security.
6. Limited career advancement opportunities: Women may find it difficult to advance in their careers due to factors such as lack of mentorship, limited networking opportunities, or unconscious bias.
7. Burnout and stress: Out-of-work women in the healthcare system may experience burnout and stress due to the demands of caring for others, managing work and family responsibilities, and navigating gender biases and discrimination.
8. Limited access to resources: Women may not have equal access to resources such as training and development opportunities, which can limit their ability to advance in their careers.
9. Stereotyping and stigma: Women in the healthcare industry may face stereotypes and stigma based on their gender, which can impact their self-esteem and confidence.
10. Limited representation in leadership positions: Women may be underrepresented in leadership positions, which can limit their opportunities for career advancement and impact their ability to influence healthcare policies and practices.
