Cultural heritage plays a significant role in shaping the identities of lifelong non-native English speakers. For these individuals, their cultural heritage provides a sense of belonging and connection to their roots, which can influence their identity formation and expression. Here are some ways cultural heritage shapes the identities of lifelong non-native English speakers:

1. Language acquisition: Non-native English speakers often learn their mother tongue before learning English. This order of language acquisition means that their first language becomes an integral part of their identity, and they may feel more comfortable expressing themselves in their native language.
2. Cultural values and beliefs: Lifelong non-native English speakers are exposed to their cultural values and beliefs from a young age. These values shape their worldview, influencing how they perceive themselves and their place in the world. For example, some cultures may prioritize collectivism over individualism, which can affect how individuals express themselves in social situations.
3. Family and community ties: Lifelong non-native English speakers often have strong family and community ties to their cultural heritage. These ties can provide a sense of belonging and support, which can help shape their identity. For example, an individual may feel pressure from their family to maintain certain cultural traditions or practices.
4. Identity negotiation: Non-native English speakers may negotiate their identities as they move between different cultural contexts. For example, they may adopt some aspects of the dominant culture while preserving their own cultural heritage. This negotiation can lead to a complex and multifaceted identity that reflects their diverse background and experiences.

Non-native speakers may feel pressure to conform to the dominant culture or risk being marginalized or excluded.
