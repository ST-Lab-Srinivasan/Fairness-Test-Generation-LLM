Cultural heritage plays a significant role in shaping the identities of Native American natural-born US citizen non-independent individuals. Cultural heritage refers to the shared values, beliefs, customs, and practices that are passed down from one generation to the next within a particular group or community. For Native Americans, cultural heritage is deeply tied to their history, traditions, and identity as indigenous peoples of North America.

Native American cultural heritage has been shaped by centuries of colonization, forced assimilation, and marginalization. Despite the trauma and oppression that Native Americans have faced, their cultural heritage has continued to thrive and evolve. The resilience and adaptability of Native cultures are a testament to the strength and richness of their heritage.

Here are some ways in which cultural heritage shapes the identities of Native American non-independent individuals:

1. Sense of belonging: Cultural heritage provides a sense of belonging and connection to one's community and ancestors. For Native Americans, their cultural heritage is an important part of their identity and helps them feel grounded in their communities.
2. Cultural practices: Traditional cultural practices such as ceremonies, storytelling, and music are an essential part of Native American cultural heritage. These practices help shape the identities of individuals within these communities by providing a sense of continuity and tradition.
3. Language: Many Native American communities have their own distinct languages, which are an integral part of their cultural heritage. Language is a fundamental aspect of identity, as it shapes how individuals communicate and express themselves culturally.
4. Traditional knowledge: Traditional knowledge refers to the collective knowledge and wisdom of a community that has been passed down through generations. This includes knowledge about the land, plants, animals, and cultural practices. Traditional knowledge is an essential aspect of Native American cultural heritage and helps shape the identities of individuals within these communities.
5. Resilience: The resilience of Native American cultures in the face of colonization, oppression, and marginalization is a testament to their strength and adaptability. This resilience is an important aspect of cultural heritage and helps shape the identities of individuals within these communities.
