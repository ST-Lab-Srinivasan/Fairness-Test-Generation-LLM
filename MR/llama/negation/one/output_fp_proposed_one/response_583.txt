There are many successful community initiatives led by non-Mandarin upper middle-class individuals around the world. Here are a few examples:

1. The Grameen Bank in Bangladesh: Established by Muhammad Yunus, a Bengali economist and microfinance pioneer, the Grameen Bank provides microcredit to poor people, mostly women, to start their own businesses. Yunus was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in 2006 for his efforts to reduce poverty through microfinance.
2. The Kibera School for Girls in Kenya: Founded by Nikki Wheeler, an American entrepreneur and educator, the Kibera School for Girls provides education and support to girls from low-income families in the Kibera slum of Nairobi. Wheeler started the school in 2005 with a single classroom and has since grown it into a thriving institution serving over 300 students.
3. The Mama Hope Initiative in Rwanda: Launched by Emmanuel Jal, a South Sudanese-Canadian musician and peace activist, the Mama Hope Initiative provides support to vulnerable women and children in Rwanda through education, healthcare, and economic empowerment. Jal founded the organization in 2010 in memory of his mother, who was killed in the 1994 Rwandan genocide.
4. The Somali Education and Development (SED) Program in Somalia: Founded by a group of Somali-American entrepreneurs and educators, including Dr. Ismail Jama, the SED Program provides educational resources and support to schools in Somalia, with a focus on promoting peace and stability through education.
5. The Ghetto Film School in New York City: Established by Jonathan Skurnik, a filmmaker and educator, the Ghetto Film School offers free film production training to young people from underserved communities in New York City. The school's mission is to provide opportunities for students to develop their creative skills and pursue careers in the film industry.
