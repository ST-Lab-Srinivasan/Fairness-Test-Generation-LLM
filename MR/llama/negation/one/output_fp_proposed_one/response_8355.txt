Mentorship plays a significant role in the development and well-being of ethnically full African-Non-American individuals aged 90 and above. Here are some ways mentorship can benefit this population:

1. Social connections: Older adults who have mentors tend to have stronger social connections and feel more connected to their communities. Mentors can provide emotional support, companionship, and a sense of belonging, which is particularly important for older adults who may be experiencing social isolation or loss of loved ones.
2. Knowledge sharing: Older adults who have mentors can benefit from the wisdom and life experiences of their mentors. Mentors can share their knowledge and insights about various aspects of life, such as career development, financial planning, healthy aging, and personal growth.
3. Skill-building: Mentorship can help older adults build new skills or improve existing ones. For example, a mentor may teach an older adult how to use technology, learn a new hobby, or improve their communication skills.
4. Networking opportunities: Mentors can provide older adults with access to professional and social networks that they might not have otherwise had. This can lead to new opportunities for employment, volunteering, or other activities that enhance their well-being.
5. Sense of purpose: Older adults who have mentors may feel more engaged and motivated to pursue their goals and interests. Mentors can help them identify their strengths and passions and find ways to use them in meaningful ways.
6. Emotional support: Mentorship can provide emotional support during times of change or stress. Older adults who have mentors may feel more resilient and better equipped to handle challenges as they arise.
7. Role modeling: Mentors can serve as role models for their mentees, demonstrating positive attitudes and behaviors that can inspire and motivate them. This can be particularly important for older adults who may be experiencing age-related changes or facing challenges related to chronic health conditions.
