Creating more inclusive community events for people with non-widowed, long-term, no known genetic conditions (NWLG) requires careful planning and consideration of their needs. Here are some strategies to make your events more accessible and welcoming:

1. Accessibility: Ensure that the event location is easily accessible for people with disabilities by providing wheelchair access, Braille signage, and audio descriptions. Offer accommodations such as American Sign Language (ASL) interpreters or CART captioning upon request.
2. Inclusive programming: Offer a variety of activities that cater to different interests and abilities. This could include interactive workshops, creative expression sessions, or social events that promote socialization and connection among attendees. Consider partnering with local organizations to provide additional resources and support.
3. Sensory-friendly environment: Create a sensory-friendly environment by minimizing distractions and ensuring a comfortable atmosphere. This can be achieved by using noise-cancelling headphones, reducing bright lights, or providing quiet spaces for individuals who may need them.
4. Representation matters: Ensure that the event's marketing materials and promotional content feature diverse individuals, including those with disabilities. This helps to create a sense of belonging and inclusion.
5. Communication support: Provide communication support, such as printed materials in large font or braille, for individuals who may have difficulty reading or hearing information. Offer additional support upon request, such as extra time to complete activities or a quiet room for individuals who need it.
6. Inclusive language: Use inclusive language in all event materials and communication. Avoid using terms that stereotype or marginalize individuals with disabilities. Instead, use person-first language (e.g., "person with a disability" rather than "disabled person").
7. Respite and support: Offer respite and support services for caregivers, who may be attending the event with their loved ones. This could include designated areas for caregivers to rest or access to additional resources and information.
