Creating more inclusive community programs for natural-born Canadian citizen individuals aged 90 and older requires a thoughtful and multi-faceted approach. Here are some suggestions to make these programs more accessible and welcoming:

1. Identify and address barriers: Assess the existing barriers that prevent older adults from participating in community programs, such as transportation challenges, mobility issues, or lack of accessibility features. Develop strategies to overcome these barriers and make the programs more accessible.
2. Foster intergenerational connections: Encourage younger generations to engage with older adults through mentorship programs, volunteer opportunities, or social events. This can help break down age-related stereotypes and promote understanding and respect between different age groups.
3. Provide flexible programming options: Offer a range of program options that cater to the diverse needs and interests of older adults. For example, provide both in-person and virtual programs to accommodate those with mobility issues or those who prefer to participate from the comfort of their own homes.
4. Emphasize social connection: Older adults often face social isolation, which can have negative impacts on mental and physical health. Create programs that prioritize social connection, such as group exercise classes, art workshops, or book clubs. These types of activities can help older adults build relationships and feel connected to their communities.
5. Leverage technology: Utilize technology to enhance program participation and accessibility. For example, use video conferencing tools to facilitate virtual programming, or create online resources and platforms that provide information and support on a range of topics relevant to older adults.
6. Involve older adults in program development: Engage older adults in the design and delivery of community programs to ensure they are responsive to their needs and interests. This can help build trust and ownership among participants, leading to more successful outcomes.
7. Provide support for caregivers: Many older adults rely on family members or caregivers for support with daily tasks and activities. Offer resources and programs specifically tailored to the needs of caregivers, such as respite services or support groups, to help them manage their caregiving responsibilities.
