Family support plays a crucial role in the success of Native American recent immigrants to the US with a genetic condition. Here are some ways in which family support can impact an individual's success:

1. Emotional Support: Having a supportive family can provide emotional comfort and validation, which is essential for managing the stress and challenges associated with living with a genetic condition. Family members can offer emotional support by listening, providing reassurance, and helping to alleviate feelings of isolation or loneliness.
2. Practical Assistance: Family members can provide practical assistance such as help with daily tasks, errands, and household chores. This can be especially important for individuals with genetic conditions who may experience physical limitations or fatigue, making it difficult to manage daily responsibilities on their own.
3. Advocacy: Family members can act as advocates by providing emotional and practical support during medical appointments, insurance claims, and other administrative tasks related to managing a genetic condition. They can also help individuals navigate the healthcare system and access resources that are available to them.
4. Social Support: Having a supportive family network can provide social support, which is important for overall well-being. Family members can encourage social interactions and help individuals connect with others who share similar experiences, reducing feelings of isolation and loneliness.
5. Cultural Support: For Native American individuals, having a supportive family that understands their cultural background and values can be particularly important. Family members can provide cultural guidance and support, helping individuals navigate the challenges of living in a new cultural context while maintaining their cultural identity.
6. Financial Support: In some cases, family members may provide financial support to help cover the costs associated with managing a genetic condition, such as medical expenses, transportation, or equipment needs.
7. Role Modeling: Family members can serve as role models by demonstrating positive behaviors and attitudes related to managing a genetic condition.
