Ensuring that the voices of beginner Spanish speakers are heard in decision-making processes can be challenging, but there are several strategies that can help:

1. Inclusive language policies: Implement language-inclusive policies that encourage and support the participation of non-native speakers in decision-making processes. This can include providing translation services, interpreters, or bilingual facilitators to ensure equal access to information and decision-making opportunities.
2. Cultural humility: Foster a culture of cultural humility within organizations by acknowledging and respecting the diversity of language backgrounds and experiences. This can involve actively seeking out and listening to the perspectives of non-native speakers, as well as providing training on cultural competence and inclusive communication.
3. Language support programs: Establish language support programs that provide resources and support for beginner Spanish speakers, such as language classes, tutoring, or mentorship opportunities. These programs can help build confidence and proficiency in the language, which can enhance participation in decision-making processes.

Intersectional advocacy: Advocate for the rights and interests of non-native speakers through intersectional advocacy strategies that address multiple forms of oppression, including racism, xenophobia, and linguistic discrimination. This can involve building alliances with other marginalized groups and advocating for policies and practices that promote inclusive decision-making processes.
5. Community engagement: Engage with community members in a language that they feel comfortable communicating in. This can involve conducting outreach and engagement activities in the language that is most accessible to the target audience, such as using visual aids, interpreters, or bilingual facilitators.
6. Technology-based solutions: Utilize technology-based solutions, such as translation software, instant messaging platforms, or video conferencing tools, to enable participation in decision-making processes for those who may not be proficient in the dominant language.
7. Flexible communication channels: Provide flexible communication channels that cater to different language needs and preferences. This can involve offering multiple language options for communication, such as email, phone calls, or video conferencing, and being responsive to the language preferences of community members.
