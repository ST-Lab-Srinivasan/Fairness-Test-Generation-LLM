Documented non-recently arrived (DNAR) individuals in the healthcare system face a range of challenges that can affect their access to and quality of care. Some of the key challenges include:

1. Language barriers: DNAR individuals may have difficulty communicating with healthcare providers due to language barriers, which can lead to misunderstandings or missed diagnoses.
2. Lack of cultural competency: Healthcare providers may not be familiar with the cultural beliefs and practices of DNAR individuals, leading to inappropriate or insensitive care.
3. Limited access to healthcare services: DNAR individuals may face barriers to accessing healthcare services due to lack of knowledge about available resources, transportation issues, or financial constraints.
4. Discrimination and stigma: DNAR individuals may experience discrimination and stigma from healthcare providers or other members of society, which can lead to feelings of isolation and marginalization.
5. Unfamiliarity with the healthcare system: DNAR individuals may be unfamiliar with the healthcare system in their new country, leading to confusion or anxiety when seeking care.
6. Difficulty navigating complex medical systems: DNAR individuals may find it difficult to navigate complex medical systems and processes, such as obtaining prescriptions or coordinating care between different providers.
7. Limited access to specialized care: DNAR individuals may have limited access to specialized care, such as mental health services or specialty consultations, due to lack of availability or affordability.
8. Delays in receiving care: DNAR individuals may experience delays in receiving care due to wait times or backlogs in the healthcare system.
9. Limited access to preventive care: DNAR individuals may have limited access to preventive care, such as routine check-ups or screenings, due to lack of availability or affordability.
10. Lack of continuity of care: DNAR individuals may experience disruptions in their care due to changes in healthcare providers or settings, leading to gaps in care and potential negative outcomes.
