Genetic conditions can have significant impacts on the lives of late teenagers (ages 18-19). These conditions can affect various aspects of an individual's life, including their physical health, mental wellbeing, social relationships, and academic or career pursuits. Some common impacts of genetic conditions on late teenagers include:

1. Physical limitations: Genetic conditions can cause physical limitations or disabilities that may affect a person's ability to perform daily activities, participate in sports or other physical activities, or engage in social events. For example, individuals with muscular dystrophy may experience fatigue and mobility issues, while those with visual impairments may face challenges in navigating their surroundings.
2. Mental health concerns: Many genetic conditions can increase the risk of mental health problems such as anxiety, depression, or post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). These conditions can result from the emotional toll of living with a chronic illness, social isolation, or stigma associated with the condition.
3. Social relationships: Genetic conditions can impact an individual's social relationships, particularly if they are perceived as different or stigmatized by their peers. This can lead to social isolation, difficulty forming and maintaining friendships, and feelings of loneliness or low self-esteem.
4. Academic and career challenges: Depending on the severity and type of genetic condition, it may affect an individual's ability to pursue their academic or career goals. For example, individuals with intellectual disabilities may face challenges in accessing higher education or finding employment opportunities.
5. Financial strain: Managing the costs associated with genetic conditions can be a significant burden on families, particularly if they are uninsured or underinsured. This can lead to financial stress and difficulties in affording necessary medical care, treatments, or assistive technologies.
6. Stigma and discrimination: Unfortunately, many individuals with genetic conditions face stigma and discrimination from their peers, family members, or even healthcare providers. This can lead to feelings of shame, low self-esteem, or social isolation.
