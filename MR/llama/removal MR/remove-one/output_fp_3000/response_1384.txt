1. Ageism: Native traditionalists often find themselves facing age-related discrimination in the workplace. As they get older, they may feel that their skills and experience are not valued or recognized, leading to limited career advancement opportunities.

Lack of flexibility: Many native traditionalists have families and other responsibilities that make it difficult for them to adapt to non-traditional work arrangements, such as telecommuting or flexible schedules. This can limit their job prospects, particularly in industries where these arrangements are becoming more common.
3. Limited exposure to new technologies: Traditionalists may not be as familiar with the latest digital tools and technologies, which can make it difficult for them to keep up with changing work requirements and expectations.
4. Difficulty in networking: Native traditionalists may struggle to build connections and networks, particularly if they are not actively seeking new opportunities. This can limit their access to job opportunities and professional development.
5. Changing workplace culture: Traditionalists may find it challenging to adapt to a changing workplace culture that emphasizes collaboration, creativity, and innovation. They may prefer a more structured and hierarchical approach to work, which can make it difficult for them to fit in with newer, more flexible work environments.
6. Lack of digital literacy: Many native traditionalists may not have the same level of comfort or familiarity with digital technologies as younger workers. This can limit their ability to navigate online job platforms, communicate with colleagues and clients remotely, and perform tasks that require strong digital skills.
