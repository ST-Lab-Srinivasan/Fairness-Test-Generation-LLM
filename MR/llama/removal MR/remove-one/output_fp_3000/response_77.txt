Creating more inclusive social policies for working-class bereaved individuals requires a comprehensive approach that addresses the unique challenges faced by this group. Here are some recommendations:

1. Acknowledge the impact of grief on work performance: The bereavement process can be emotionally and physically draining, making it difficult for working-class individuals to perform their job duties effectively. Employers should be understanding and accommodating, allowing for flexible work arrangements or temporary leave without penalty.
2. Provide access to employee assistance programs (EAPs): EAPs offer counseling and other support services to help employees cope with grief. These programs can be particularly helpful for working-class individuals who may not have the financial resources to seek private therapy.
3. Offer financial support: Financial burdens can exacerbate the difficulties faced by bereaved individuals. Employers could provide additional pay or a one-time payment to help cover funeral expenses, lost wages, or other financial obligations related to the death of a loved one.
4. Promote workplace diversity and inclusion: Creating a more inclusive work environment can help working-class bereaved individuals feel supported and valued. This can involve providing training on cultural sensitivity, offering flexible work arrangements, and promoting diversity and inclusion in hiring practices.
5. Encourage open communication: Bereavement can be a taboo topic in some workplaces, making it difficult for employees to discuss their experiences. Employers should foster an environment where employees feel comfortable talking about their grief and accessing support when needed.
6. Develop a bereavement policy: Having a clear and comprehensive bereavement policy can help ensure that working-class individuals receive the support they need during this difficult time. The policy should outline eligibility for leave, payment options, and any other benefits available to employees.
7. Offer mental health resources: Working-class individuals may have limited access to mental health resources due to financial constraints or lack of knowledge about available services. Employers could provide information on low-cost or free mental health resources, such as counseling services or hotlines.
