Documented non-citizens, also known as legal permanent residents or LPRs, face a range of challenges when accessing healthcare in the United States. Here are some of the common challenges they encounter:

1. Language barriers: Many documented non-citizens may not speak English fluently, which can create difficulties when communicating with healthcare providers and understanding medical instructions.
2. Limited access to care: Documented non-citizens may face barriers in accessing healthcare due to limitations on their eligibility for certain programs such as Medicaid or the Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP). They may also face difficulties in finding providers who accept their insurance.
3. Cost and affordability: Documented non-citizens may struggle to pay for healthcare services due to limited financial resources. They may also face challenges in affording copays, deductibles, and other out-of-pocket expenses.
4. Difficulty navigating the healthcare system: The complexities of the U.S. healthcare system can be overwhelming for anyone, let alone for documented non-citizens who may face additional barriers in accessing care. They may have limited knowledge of the system and its various programs, making it difficult to navigate and access the care they need.
5. Discrimination: Unfortunately, documented non-citizens may face discrimination when seeking healthcare services. Some providers may be hesitant to treat them due to their immigration status, which can result in delayed or inadequate care.
6. Limited cultural competency: Healthcare providers may not always be aware of the specific health beliefs and practices of documented non-citizens, leading to miscommunication or misunderstandings. This can result in inadequate care or missed diagnoses.
7. Lack of culturally competent providers: Documented non-citizens may face challenges in finding providers who are knowledgeable about their cultural beliefs and practices, which can affect the quality of care they receive.
8. Transportation barriers: Some documented non-citizens may lack access to reliable transportation, making it difficult to get to appointments or pick up prescriptions.
