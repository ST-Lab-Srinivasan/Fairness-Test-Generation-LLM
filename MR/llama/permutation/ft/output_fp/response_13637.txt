Addressing the needs of mentally disabled Native American natural-born US citizens in disaster response requires a comprehensive approach that considers their unique cultural, social, and historical context. Here are some strategies to consider:

1. Inclusive Disaster Planning: Involve mental health professionals, disability organizations, and individuals with mental disabilities in disaster planning and preparedness efforts. Ensure that their needs and concerns are addressed in emergency response plans.
2. Accessible Communication: Provide accessible communication methods, such as American Sign Language (ASL) interpreters or written materials in braille, for individuals who are deaf or hard of hearing. This can help ensure that critical information is communicated effectively during a disaster.
3. Safe Evacuation: Plan evacuation routes and shelter locations that are accessible to individuals with mobility impairments. Provide additional support, such as extra time to evacuate or assistance from trained staff, as needed.
4. Adaptive Equipment: Provide adaptive equipment, such as wheelchairs, walkers, or oxygen tanks, for individuals who require them during a disaster. Ensure that these items are available and accessible at shelters or evacuation sites.
5. Mental Health Support: Offer mental health support services, such as counseling or therapy, to help individuals cope with the stress of a disaster. Be aware of cultural differences in mental health and provide culturally sensitive support.
6. Cultural Competency Training: Provide cultural competency training for emergency responders to better understand the unique needs and cultural practices of Native American communities. This can help ensure that response efforts are respectful and effective.
7. Community-Based Response: Involve community members in disaster response efforts, such as volunteer teams or community-led evacuation planning. This can help build trust and increase the effectiveness of response efforts.
8. Inclusive Sheltering: Ensure that shelters are accessible and inclusive for individuals with mental disabilities. Provide private spaces for those who may need quiet time, as well as accessible amenities such as bathrooms or food services.
