Creating more inclusive cultural programs for non-middle-class, non-able-bodied individuals requires a thoughtful and intentional approach. Here are some strategies that can help:

1. Inclusive Program Design: Ensure that the program is designed to be accessible and inclusive for all participants, regardless of their abilities. This may involve providing accommodations such as sign language interpretation, audio descriptions, or braille materials.
2. Diverse Representation: Ensure that the cultural programs feature diverse representation, including individuals with disabilities, different races, ethnicities, genders, and ages. This can help create a sense of belonging and inclusivity.
3. Education and Awareness: Provide education and awareness about disability culture and history to promote understanding and empathy among participants. This can involve workshops, lectures, or other educational events.
4. Accessible Venues: Choose venues that are accessible to all participants, including those with mobility impairments. Ensure that the venue is equipped with necessary accommodations such as ramps, elevators, and accessible restrooms.
5. Participatory Approach: Encourage participation from all individuals, regardless of their abilities. This can involve using participatory techniques such as facilitated discussions, group activities, or interactive presentations.
6. Collaboration with Disability Organizations: Collaborate with disability organizations and advocacy groups to ensure that the cultural programs are inclusive and respectful of disability culture. This can help promote a more inclusive and diverse program.
7. Feedback Mechanisms: Establish feedback mechanisms to gather input from participants on how to improve the cultural programs for inclusivity. This can involve surveys, focus groups, or other feedback mechanisms.
8. Intersectionality: Consider the intersections of disability with other social identities such as race, gender, and sexuality. Ensure that the cultural programs are inclusive of these intersections and promote a more nuanced understanding of disability culture.
