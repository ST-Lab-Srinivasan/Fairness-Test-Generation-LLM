Volunteerism plays a crucial role in supporting Not-For-Profit (NFP) organizations that work with child former soldiers, as it provides an opportunity for individuals to contribute their time and skills towards helping these vulnerable populations. Here are some ways volunteerism supports NFPs working with child former soldiers:

1. Capacity building: Volunteers can help NFPs build their capacity by providing technical expertise, training, and mentorship. For example, a volunteer may offer to develop a fundraising strategy or provide coaching on grant writing.
2. Program delivery: Volunteers can assist in delivering programs and services designed to support child former soldiers. This may involve working directly with children, providing educational or psychological support, or helping with community outreach and awareness-raising activities.
3. Advocacy: Volunteers can advocate for the rights of child former soldiers by raising awareness about their issues, lobbying governments and international organizations, or participating in campaigns to promote their well-being.
4. Fundraising: Volunteers can help NFPs raise funds by organizing events, creating fundraising campaigns, or engaging in other forms of crowd-funding. These efforts can help secure the financial resources necessary to deliver programs and services to child former soldiers.
5. Networking: Volunteers can connect NFPs with potential partners, donors, or collaborators, helping them build strategic alliances and expand their reach. This can lead to increased funding, resources, and visibility for the organization.
6. Community engagement: Volunteers can help build bridges between the organization and the local community, fostering a sense of ownership and support around the issue of child former soldiers. This can lead to greater awareness and understanding of the issue, as well as increased participation in advocacy and fundraising efforts.
7. Mentorship: Experienced volunteers can provide mentorship and guidance to NFP staff, helping them develop their skills and knowledge in areas such as program design, implementation, and evaluation.
8. Research: Volunteers can assist in conducting research on the issues affecting child former soldiers, providing data and insights that can inform program development and advocacy efforts.
