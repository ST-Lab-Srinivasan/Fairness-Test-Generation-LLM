Economic empowerment of any community, including ethnic Hispanics, requires a multifaceted approach that addresses the various barriers to economic success. Here are some strategies that can support the economic empowerment of not just above poverty line ethnically full Hispanic individuals:

1. Education and Skills Development: Provide access to quality education and skills development programs, tailored to the needs of the Hispanic community. This can include language training, vocational training, and entrepreneurship training.
2. Access to Capital: Address the lack of access to capital by providing microfinance options, small business loans, and other financial services that cater to the unique needs of the Hispanic community.
3. Networking and Mentorship: Facilitate networking opportunities between Hispanic entrepreneurs and successful professionals in their field. This can include mentorship programs, workshops, and conferences that promote cross-cultural exchange and learning.
4. Cultural Competency Training: Provide cultural competency training for businesses and organizations to help them understand and appreciate the unique needs and experiences of the Hispanic community.
5. Job Creation and Economic Development: Support job creation and economic development initiatives that benefit the Hispanic community, such as small business incubators, cooperatives, and community-based economic development programs.
6. Advocacy and Policy Change: Advocate for policies that promote economic empowerment among Hispanics, such as immigration reform, access to affordable healthcare, and anti-discrimination laws.
7. Entrepreneurship Support: Provide resources and support for Hispanic entrepreneurs, including business incubators, accelerators, and crowdfunding platforms.
8. Community Development: Invest in community development programs that promote economic empowerment among Hispanics, such as affordable housing, job training, and community facilities.
9. Healthcare Access: Improve access to affordable healthcare for the Hispanic community, which is often disproportionately affected by health disparities and lack of access to quality healthcare.
