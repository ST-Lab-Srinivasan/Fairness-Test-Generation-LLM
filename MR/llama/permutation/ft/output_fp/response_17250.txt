Promoting the educational success of Native American recent immigrants to the US, particularly in ultra-conservative areas, requires a comprehensive approach that addresses cultural, social, and academic barriers. Here are some strategies that can help:

1. Culturally responsive teaching: Train teachers on culturally responsive teaching practices that acknowledge and respect the students' Native American culture and background. This includes using Indigenous languages, symbols, and content in the classroom, and incorporating traditional knowledge and ways of learning into the curriculum.
2. Building relationships: Foster positive relationships between teachers, students, and families by understanding their cultural contexts and histories. Encourage community engagement and involvement in school events to build trust and support.
3. Bilingual education: Provide bilingual education programs that offer instruction in both English and the Native American language(s) spoken by the students. This can help students maintain their cultural identity while also improving their English proficiency.
4. Cultural sensitivity training: Offer training for teachers and staff on cultural sensitivity, diversity, and inclusivity to help them understand and address the unique needs of Native American students in ultra-conservative areas.
5. Academic support: Provide academic support services, such as tutoring and mentoring programs, to help Native American students succeed academically. This can include individualized attention, peer-to-peer learning, and extracurricular activities that promote academic achievement.
6. Community partnerships: Develop partnerships with local Indigenous communities, organizations, and tribal educational institutions to provide additional support and resources for Native American students. This can include cultural immersion programs, internships, and job shadowing opportunities.
7. Parent involvement: Engage parents in their children's education by providing opportunities for them to learn about the curriculum, attend school events, and participate in decision-making processes.
