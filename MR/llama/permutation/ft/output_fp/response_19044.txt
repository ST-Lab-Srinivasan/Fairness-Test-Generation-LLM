Cultural heritage plays a significant role in the lives of Native American natural-born US citizens, as it is an integral part of their identity, history, and community. Here are some ways cultural heritage shapes the lives of these individuals:

1. Sense of belonging: Cultural heritage provides a sense of belonging to a particular group or community, which is essential for mental and emotional well-being. For Native American natural-born US citizens, their cultural heritage connects them to their ancestors, traditions, and the land.
2. Identity formation: Cultural heritage helps shape an individual's identity, including their values, beliefs, and practices. It influences how they perceive themselves in relation to their community, society, and the world around them.
3. Tradition and customs: Native American cultural heritage includes a rich tapestry of traditions and customs, such as ceremonies, dances, music, and art. These practices are an essential part of daily life and are passed down from generation to generation.
4. Language: Many Native American communities have their own distinct languages, which are crucial for preserving cultural heritage. Language is a vital tool for communication, storytelling, and cultural expression.
5. Resilience and adaptability: Cultural heritage has helped Native American communities survive and thrive despite historical trauma, oppression, and marginalization. It has enabled them to adapt to changing circumstances while maintaining their unique identity and way of life.
6. Connection to the land: For many Native American communities, cultural heritage is closely tied to the land and natural resources. Their connection to the land and its rhythms is essential for their spiritual, emotional, and physical well-being.
7. Preservation of history: Cultural heritage serves as a means of preserving historical events, traditions, and practices that are vital to understanding Native American communities' experiences and perspectives. It helps to challenge dominant narratives and promote a more accurate representation of their history.
8. Intergenerational relationships: Cultural heritage fosters strong intergenerational relationships within Native American communities. Elders and younger generations learn from each other, sharing knowledge, traditions, and practices that are essential for maintaining cultural identity.
