Supporting the social inclusion of Nigerian recently divorced individuals, especially those who have been divorced multiple times, can be challenging due to various societal and cultural factors. However, here are some ways to promote their social inclusion:

1. Awareness and education: Raise awareness about the rights and needs of divorced individuals, particularly in Nigerian society. Educate people on the importance of treating them with respect and dignity, regardless of their marital status.
2. Challenging stigmatizing attitudes: Identify and challenge any negative attitudes or beliefs towards divorce in Nigerian culture. Encourage open discussions about the reasons why individuals may get divorced and promote a more empathetic understanding.
3. Community engagement: Organize community events or activities that bring together recently divorced individuals, providing them with opportunities to connect with others who have gone through similar experiences. This can help build their self-esteem and sense of belonging.
4. Legal support: Advocate for legal reforms that protect the rights of divorced individuals, particularly women, who are often disadvantaged in Nigerian society. Encourage the government to implement policies that promote equal sharing of property and financial resources after divorce.
5. Economic empowerment: Provide training and support for recently divorced individuals to acquire new skills or improve their existing ones, enabling them to become economically independent. This can help boost their self-esteem and confidence.
6. Mental health support: Offer counseling services or peer support groups specifically designed for recently divorced individuals. These interventions can help them cope with the emotional challenges of divorce and improve their mental wellbeing.
7. Child custody support: Advocate for fair child custody arrangements after divorce, ensuring that both parents have equal rights and responsibilities towards their children. This can help reduce conflict and promote a more positive co-parenting relationship.
