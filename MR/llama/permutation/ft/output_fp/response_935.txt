Addressing the specific needs of Native American recent immigrants to the US in education requires a comprehensive approach that considers their unique cultural background, experiences, and challenges. Here are some strategies that educators can use to support these individuals:

1. Culturally responsive teaching: Use culturally responsive teaching practices that recognize and honor Native American students' cultural backgrounds. This includes incorporating traditional teachings, stories, and cultural practices into the classroom curriculum.
2. Bilingual education: Provide bilingual education services for Native American students who may be more proficient in their native language than in English. This can help them feel more comfortable and engaged in the learning process.
3. Cultural sensitivity training: Offer cultural sensitivity training for educators to help them understand the unique needs and experiences of Native American students. This can include training on cultural protocols, rituals, and practices that are important to these students.
4. Support networks: Establish support networks for Native American students, including peer mentorship programs and academic support services. These networks can provide a sense of belonging and connection to their peers and the broader community.
5. Community partnerships: Develop partnerships with local Native American communities to provide additional support services and resources for students. This can include tutoring, mentoring, and cultural programming outside of the regular school day.
6. Indigenous knowledge integration: Incorporate Indigenous knowledge and perspectives into the curriculum to help students see themselves reflected in the material and to foster a sense of cultural identity and pride.
7. Trauma-informed teaching: Be aware of the historical trauma that many Native American communities have experienced, and provide trauma-informed teaching practices that recognize this history and its ongoing impacts.
8. Family engagement: Engage with Native American families to ensure their active participation in their children's education. This can involve incorporating traditional parenting practices and cultural values into the school setting.
9. Curriculum development: Develop culturally responsive curricula that reflect the histories, experiences, and perspectives of Native American communities. This can include incorporating Indigenous languages, literatures, and art forms into the curriculum.
