Addressing the specific needs of ethnically full African-American lifelong recently single individuals in education requires a comprehensive approach that considers their unique experiences, challenges, and opportunities. Here are some strategies that can help address their needs:

1. Culturally responsive teaching: Ensure that educators are trained to understand the cultural backgrounds of African-American students and how those cultures impact their learning experiences. This includes being aware of the historical trauma and systemic racism that has affected the community, and how those factors influence student behaviors and academic performance.
2. Inclusive curriculum: Develop an inclusive curriculum that reflects the experiences and histories of African-American students. This can include incorporating literature, art, music, and other cultural expressions from the African-American community into the curriculum.
3. Supportive policies: Implement policies that support the academic success of African-American students, such as mentorship programs, academic support services, and scholarships specifically designed for this group.
4. Safe spaces: Create safe and inclusive learning environments where African-American students feel comfortable expressing themselves and sharing their experiences without fear of judgment or discrimination.
5. Teacher training: Provide ongoing professional development for educators to learn about the cultural nuances of the African-American community, how to address microaggressions in the classroom, and how to create inclusive learning environments.
6. Community partnerships: Foster partnerships with local African-American communities to provide internship opportunities, mentorship programs, and other real-world experiences that can help students connect their academic studies to real-life situations.
7. Identity-based education: Offer courses or workshops that focus on the history, culture, and identity of the African-American community. This can help students develop a positive sense of self and understand how their identity intersects with their academic experiences.
8. Mental health support: Provide mental health support services specifically designed for African-American students who may face unique challenges related to systemic racism, discrimination, and historical trauma.
9. Family engagement: Engage with the families of African-American students through regular communication, workshops, and other events that promote collaboration and mutual support.
