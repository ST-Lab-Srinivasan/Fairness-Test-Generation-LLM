Economic empowerment is essential for any individual or community to reach their full potential, and it's especially important for marginalized groups like African Americans. Here are some strategies to support the economic empowerment of not just above poverty line mixed African Americans:

1. Access to Capital: Provide access to capital through loans, grants, or investments that can help them start or grow their businesses. This can be done through organizations like the U.S. Small Business Administration (SBA), the Minority Business Enterprise (MBE) program, or community development financial institutions (CDFI).
2. Entrepreneurship Training: Offer training and resources to help them develop entrepreneurial skills, such as business planning, marketing, and financial management. This can be done through organizations like the Small Business Administration (SBA), the National Black Chamber of Commerce, or the African American Chamber of Commerce.
3. Networking Opportunities: Create networking opportunities that connect them with potential investors, customers, and partners. This can be done through events like conferences, trade shows, or pitch competitions.
4. Mentorship Programs: Develop mentorship programs that pair them with successful entrepreneurs or professionals who can provide guidance and support. This can be done through organizations like SCORE, the National Black Chamber of Commerce, or the African American Chamber of Commerce.
5. Skill-Building Workshops: Offer workshops and training sessions that help them develop new skills, such as digital marketing, accounting, or project management. This can be done through organizations like General Assembly, Udacity, or Coursera.
6. Access to Markets: Provide access to markets through partnerships with major corporations, government agencies, or other organizations that can help them access new customers and revenue streams.
7. Advocacy and Policy Change: Advocate for policies that promote economic empowerment for African Americans, such as the Small Business Jobs Act, the Black Business List, or the National Black Chamber of Commerce's policy platform.
