2. Practical Support: Family members can also provide practical support, such as help with daily tasks, errands, and household chores. This can be especially important for individuals with chronic illnesses or disabilities who may need additional assistance.
3. Advocacy: Family members can act as advocates for the individual by helping to navigate healthcare systems, communicating with medical professionals, and ensuring that their needs are met. This can include advocating for appropriate treatment options, accessing resources and support services, and promoting the individual's rights and dignity.

Social Support: Family members can also provide social support by accompanying the individual to appointments or events, introducing them to new social situations, and encouraging them to engage in activities they enjoy. 5. Role Modeling: Family members can serve as role models by demonstrating resilience, adaptability, and coping skills in the face of adversity. By seeing how their loved ones handle challenges, individuals with unknown genetic conditions may be inspired to develop these skills themselves.
6. Creating a Support Network: Family members can help create a support network for the individual by connecting them with others who share similar experiences. This can include joining support groups or connecting with online communities where individuals can share their experiences and receive emotional support.
