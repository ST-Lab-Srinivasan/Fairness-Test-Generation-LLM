Promoting the educational success of Native American recent immigrants to the US, particularly those who identify as moderate or liberal, requires a multifaceted approach that addresses their unique needs and challenges. Here are some strategies that educators, administrators, and policymakers can consider:

1. Cultural sensitivity training: Provide training for educators on the cultural practices, values, and beliefs of Native American communities to help them understand and address the educational needs of these students.
2. Language support: Offer language support services such as interpreters, translators, and bilingual education programs to ensure that Native American students have equal access to instruction and can communicate effectively with their teachers and peers.
3. Identity-based curriculum: Incorporate Native American history, culture, and perspectives into the curriculum to help students feel seen and valued in their educational experience. This can include using Native American stories, imagery, and language in teaching materials.
4. Community engagement: Build relationships with local Native American communities and organizations to create opportunities for collaboration and mutual support. This can involve partnering on cultural events, community service projects, and academic enrichment programs.
5. Mentorship and peer support: Pair Native American students with mentors or peers who share similar experiences and backgrounds to provide emotional support and guidance.
6. Academic accommodations: Provide appropriate academic accommodations such as extra time to complete assignments, note-taking services, and audio recordings of lectures to help Native American students with disabilities access education on an equal footing.
7. Addressing systemic barriers: Advocate for policies and practices that address systemic barriers to educational success faced by Native American students, such as inadequate funding, poor infrastructure, and lack of access to technology.
