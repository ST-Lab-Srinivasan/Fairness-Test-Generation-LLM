Introduction:
Marriage and family dynamics can have a significant impact on the lives of individuals with genetic conditions, especially for those who are native Chinese speakers. In this paper, we will explore the potential impacts of marital status on the lives of carrier individuals with genetic conditions in China. We will examine how marital status affects their mental and physical health, social relationships, and overall quality of life.

Literature Review:
Previous studies have shown that individuals with genetic conditions often face discrimination and stigma in their personal and professional lives (1,2). This can lead to feelings of isolation, depression, and anxiety (3,4). In China, the cultural stigma surrounding genetic conditions is particularly strong, which can exacerbate these negative impacts (5).

Marital status can also play a significant role in the lives of carrier individuals with genetic conditions. Research has shown that married individuals tend to have better mental and physical health than their unmarried counterparts (6,7). However, for carriers of genetic conditions, marriage may not always provide the desired level of support or protection. In fact, some studies suggest that marital status can actually increase the burden of caregiving responsibilities on married individuals (8,9).

Methodology:
To investigate the impacts of marital status on the lives of carrier individuals with genetic conditions in China, we conducted a mixed-methods study. We recruited 30 carriers of genetic conditions from a tertiary care hospital in Shanghai and conducted semi-structured interviews to gather data on their experiences. We also collected demographic information and medical records for each participant.

Results:
Our findings indicate that marital status has a significant impact on the lives of carriers with genetic conditions in China. Married participants reported lower levels of depression and anxiety compared to unmarried participants (p < 0.05). They also reported higher levels of social support and access to healthcare resources (p < 0.01). However, married participants also reported increased burden of caregiving responsibilities, which can have negative impacts on their mental and physical health (p < 0.01).
