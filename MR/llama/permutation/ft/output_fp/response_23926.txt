Promoting the accessibility of public services for early teenagers (13-15 years old) from upper-upper middle-income families requires a multi-faceted approach that addresses various factors influencing their access to these services. Here are some strategies that can be implemented:

1. Outreach and Awareness Programs: Organize outreach programs in schools, communities, and social events to educate young people about the available public services, such as healthcare, education, and recreational facilities. This can be done through interactive presentations, workshops, or group discussions.
2. Digital Inclusion: Create digital platforms and mobile applications that provide easy access to information on public services for early teenagers. These platforms can offer a user-friendly interface, interactive content, and filters to help young people find the most relevant services based on their interests and location.
3. Mentorship Programs: Establish mentorship programs where young people can be paired with older, more experienced individuals who can guide them in navigating public services. This can include mentors from local government agencies, community organizations, or professional networks.
4. Service-Learning Opportunities: Offer service-learning opportunities that allow young people to engage in volunteer work and contribute to their communities. These experiences can help them develop a sense of social responsibility and civic engagement, while also providing them with hands-on experience navigating public services.
5. Family Engagement: Encourage parents or guardians to play an active role in promoting the accessibility of public services for their children. This can involve educating them about the available services, supporting their children's participation in mentorship programs or service-learning opportunities, and advocating for policies that prioritize young people's needs.
6. Partnerships with Local Organizations: Collaborate with local organizations, such as youth centers, community centers, or schools, to promote the accessibility of public services. These partnerships can help leverage resources, expertise, and networks to reach a wider audience of young people.
