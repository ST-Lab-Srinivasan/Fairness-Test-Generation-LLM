Low-earning passport holders, particularly those from developing countries, face significant challenges when accessing healthcare services. Some of the common challenges include:

1. Limited financial resources: Individuals with low incomes may struggle to pay for medical expenses, including deductibles, coinsurance, and copays. This can lead to delayed or foregone care, which can exacerbate health problems and create additional healthcare needs.
2. Lack of health insurance: Many low-earning individuals do not have access to employer-based health insurance or cannot afford private insurance. This leaves them vulnerable to high out-of-pocket costs for medical care.
3. Limited access to care: In some countries, low-income individuals may face barriers to accessing healthcare services due to a lack of healthcare providers in their area, long wait times, or limited availability of specialized care.
4. Discrimination and stigma: Individuals with low incomes may experience discrimination or stigma when seeking healthcare, which can affect their mental health and well-being.
5. Limited cultural competency: Healthcare providers may not be familiar with the cultural beliefs and practices of low-income individuals, leading to misunderstandings or miscommunications that can impact care.
6. Language barriers: Individuals who are non-native speakers of the dominant language in their country may face challenges communicating with healthcare providers, which can lead to misunderstandings or delays in care.
7. Limited transportation options: In some areas, low-income individuals may not have access to reliable transportation, making it difficult to get to appointments or pick up prescriptions.
8. Complex administrative tasks: Navigating the healthcare system can be complex and time-consuming, particularly for individuals with limited education or English proficiency. This can lead to delays in care or missed opportunities for preventive services.
9. Limited availability of specialized care: In some areas, low-income individuals may not have access to specialized care, such as mental health services or complex surgeries, due to limited availability or affordability.
