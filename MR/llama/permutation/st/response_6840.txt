Introduction:

Arabic is one of the most widely spoken languages in the world, with over 300 million speakers across the globe. In Nigeria, there are several ethnic groups that speak Arabic, including the Hausa and Fulani people. However, despite its widespread use, Arabic language faces several challenges, particularly among beginner speakers in the healthcare system. This paper aims to identify and discuss these challenges faced by beginner Arabic speaker Nigerian people in the healthcare system.

Challenges faced by beginner Arabic speakers in the healthcare system:

1. Language barrier: One of the major challenges faced by beginner Arabic speakers in the healthcare system is the language barrier. Many Nigerians who speak Arabic as a second language may struggle to communicate effectively with healthcare providers, leading to misunderstandings and misdiagnosis.
2. Limited medical vocabulary: Arabic has a limited medical vocabulary compared to other languages, which can make it difficult for beginner speakers to understand and communicate about medical conditions and treatments.
3. Cultural differences: Arabic culture is different from the dominant cultures in Nigeria, which can lead to misunderstandings and miscommunications between healthcare providers and patients who speak Arabic. For example, in some Arab cultures, it is considered impolite to ask about a patient's medical history directly, while in other cultures, this is seen as necessary for proper diagnosis and treatment.
4. Limited access to healthcare resources: Many areas of Nigeria where Arabic is spoken have limited access to healthcare resources, including medical facilities, equipment, and trained healthcare providers. This can make it difficult for beginner Arabic speakers to receive proper medical care.
5. Stigma associated with speaking Arabic: In some parts of Nigeria, there is a stigma associated with speaking Arabic, which can discourage people from seeking medical care in Arabic.
6. Lack of Arabic language training for healthcare providers: Many healthcare providers in Nigeria may not receive training in Arabic, which can make it difficult for them to communicate effectively with patients who speak the language.
