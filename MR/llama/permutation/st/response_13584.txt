Permanent residents who have recently gone through a divorce, especially those who have been divorced multiple times, may face unique challenges when accessing healthcare services. Some of the common challenges faced by this group include:

1. Lack of health insurance: Divorce can lead to a loss of health insurance coverage, particularly if the former spouse was the primary breadwinner and had access to employer-based insurance. Permanent residents may struggle to afford healthcare services without adequate insurance coverage.
2. Limited access to specialized care: Divorce can also limit access to specialized care, such as mental health services or fertility treatments, which may have been available through a former spouse's insurance plan. Permanent residents may need to find alternative providers or pay out-of-pocket for these services.
3. Difficulty navigating the healthcare system: Divorce can create additional stress and complexity in managing healthcare needs, particularly if there are children involved or if one partner has a chronic condition. Permanent residents may find it challenging to navigate the healthcare system, including finding providers, understanding insurance coverage, and coordinating care for themselves and their families.
4. Financial burdens: Divorce can lead to financial strain, particularly if one partner had primary responsibility for managing finances during the marriage. Permanent residents may face increased costs for healthcare services, including co-pays, deductibles, and prescription medications, which can be a significant burden on their already limited budgets.
5. Emotional toll: Divorce can take an emotional toll on individuals, particularly if there are children involved or if the marriage ended acrimoniously. Permanent residents may struggle with feelings of loss, grief, and anxiety, which can impact their ability to manage their healthcare needs and access necessary services.
6. Limited social support: Divorce can lead to a decrease in social support networks, including friends, family, and community resources. Permanent residents may find it challenging to access emotional support or connect with others who understand their experiences, which can further complicate their ability to manage healthcare needs.
