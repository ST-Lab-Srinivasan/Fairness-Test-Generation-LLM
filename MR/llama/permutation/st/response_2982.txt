Widowed individuals, particularly those who have been widowed for an extended period of time, may face various challenges when trying to re-enter the job market. Here are some of the common difficulties they might encounter:

1. Stigma and social isolation: Widows may be seen as a burden or a problem by potential employers, especially if they have been out of the workforce for an extended period. This can lead to feelings of isolation and stigma, making it harder to find and keep a job.
2. Lack of skills and knowledge: As a result of taking care of family responsibilities or not having access to training opportunities during their marriage, widows may feel that they lack the necessary skills and knowledge to compete in the job market.
3. Limited job opportunities: Widows may find it challenging to secure a job that matches their qualifications and experience, especially if they have been out of the workforce for an extended period. This can be due to ageism, sexism, or other forms of discrimination.
4. Childcare responsibilities: Many widows have to take care of young children, which can limit their ability to work outside the home or make it difficult to balance work and family obligations.
5. Financial constraints: Widows may face financial challenges due to the loss of their partner's income, leading them to struggle to make ends meet. This can make it harder for them to invest in education or job search resources.
6. Emotional difficulties: The grieving process can be intense and prolonged, affecting a widow's mental health and ability to function in the workplace. Depression, anxiety, and other emotional challenges can make it harder for them to find and keep a job.
7. Lack of support: Widows may feel that they are alone in their struggles, leading to feelings of isolation and disconnection from society. This lack of social support can make it harder for them to navigate the job market.
8. Limited access to resources: Widows may not have access to resources such as computers, internet, or transportation, which can limit their ability to find and apply for jobs, attend interviews, or negotiate work arrangements.
