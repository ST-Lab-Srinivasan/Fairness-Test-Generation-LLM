Addressing the Specific Needs of Mixed-American Heritiage Combat Long-Time Veterans in Education:

Introduction:
Mixed-American heritage combat long-time veterans face unique challenges in their educational journey. These individuals have served their country for an extended period, often experiencing trauma and struggling to readjust to civilian life. As educators, it is essential to understand the specific needs of these individuals and provide tailored support to ensure their academic success. This paper will explore the challenges faced by mixed-American heritage combat long-time veterans in education and offer recommendations for addressing their specific needs.

Challenges Faced by Mixed-American Heritiage Combat Long-Time Veterans:

1. Identity issues: Mixed-American heritage individuals often struggle with their identity, feeling caught between different cultural worlds. This can lead to feelings of isolation and disconnection from their academic environment.
2. Trauma: Combat veterans have experienced traumatic events that can affect their mental health, social interactions, and academic performance. They may feel overwhelmed by the demands of academia, leading to anxiety or depression.
3. Adjusting to civilian life: After serving in the military, veterans face a significant adjustment period as they readjust to civilian life. This can lead to challenges in managing their time and responsibilities, maintaining relationships, and finding purpose and meaning outside of their military service.
4. Access to resources: Veterans may face barriers when accessing educational resources, such as financial aid, academic support services, and accommodations for disabilities related to their military service.
5. Stigma and discrimination: Mixed-American heritage individuals and combat veterans may experience stigma and discrimination in academic settings, leading to feelings of marginalization and exclusion.
