Promoting the educational success of Native American natural-born US citizens, particularly those who are moderately achieving, requires a multifaceted approach that addresses cultural, social, and academic factors. Here are some strategies that educators, policymakers, and parents can implement:

1. Culturally responsive teaching: Train teachers to incorporate Native American culture and traditions into the classroom curriculum. This can help students feel more connected to their heritage and education.
2. Indigenous language revitalization: Support the revitalization of Native American languages, which are critical for cultural preservation and academic success. Provide language immersion programs, language classes, or hire linguists to work with schools.
3. Mentorship and role models: Pair moderately achieving Native American students with successful Native American mentors who can inspire and guide them towards academic success.
4. Cultural events and activities: Organize cultural events and activities that promote Native American traditions, such as powwows, storytelling sessions, or traditional food cooking demonstrations. These events can help students feel more engaged and motivated in their education.
5. Community partnerships: Develop partnerships with local Native American communities to provide academic support, cultural enrichment, and career development opportunities for students.

Addressing systemic barriers: Identify and address systemic barriers that may hinder the academic success of Native American students, such as inadequate resources, lack of representation, or discrimination.
