1. Cultural norms and values: Non-native English speakers bring their own cultural norms and values to their interactions with others. These may differ from those in the dominant culture, leading to misunderstandings or miscommunications. For example, in some cultures, direct eye contact is seen as disrespectful, while in others it's a sign of confidence.
2. Language learning: Non-native English speakers may face challenges in learning and using English, particularly if their first language is different from the one they are learning. This can lead to feelings of frustration or inadequacy, which can impact their identity.
3. Identity formation: Cultural heritage can influence how non-native English speakers form their identities, including their sense of self and belonging. For example, some individuals may feel more connected to their cultural heritage than to the dominant culture, leading to a complex or divided identity.
4. Intersectionality: Non-native English speakers may experience intersecting identities based on factors such as race, ethnicity, gender, sexual orientation, and socioeconomic status. Cultural heritage can play a role in shaping these intersections and influencing how individuals navigate multiple identities.
5. Power dynamics: Non-native English speakers may face power imbalances in their interactions with others, particularly if they are from marginalized groups. Cultural heritage can impact how individuals negotiate these power dynamics and assert their identities.
