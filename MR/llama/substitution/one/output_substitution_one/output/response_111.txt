Creating more inclusive cultural events for single, multiple times civilian individuals requires a thoughtful and intentional approach. Here are some suggestions to make your events more accessible and welcoming:

1. Accessibility: Ensure that the event location is wheelchair-accessible and has accessible restrooms. Provide audio descriptions or sign language interpretation for deaf or hard of hearing attendees. Offer flexible seating options, such as seating areas with wheelchair-accessible spaces or blankets for those who prefer to sit on the floor.
2. Inclusive programming: Offer a diverse range of activities and performances that cater to different interests and abilities. For example, include sign language interpretation for concerts or dance performances, or provide audio descriptions for visual art exhibitions. Consider including interactive elements, such as workshops or hands-on activities, to engage attendees with different learning styles.
3. Representation: Ensure that the event features a diverse range of performers, artists, and speakers who reflect the community you are serving. This can include people with disabilities, different genders, races, and ages. Provide opportunities for marginalized voices to be heard through workshops, panels, or performances.
4. Communication: Clearly communicate event details, including accessibility information, programming, and performance times, through multiple channels (e.g., social media, email, phone, text messaging). Provide an accessible event schedule or program that includes important information, such as start times, locations, and accessibility features.
5. Support: Offer support to attendees who may need assistance during the event, such as escorts for people with mobility impairments or communication supports for those who are deaf or hard of hearing. Provide a quiet room or area for attendees who may become overwhelmed or need a break from the activity.
6. Advance planning: Plan the event well in advance to ensure that you can accommodate attendee needs and preferences. Consider holding an accessibility planning meeting with stakeholders, including attendees with disabilities, to gather feedback and suggestions for improvement.
