Family support plays a crucial role in the success of very young (90s+) not able-bodied people. Here are some ways in which family support can help:

1. Emotional Support: Family members can provide emotional support by listening to their needs, validating their feelings, and offering encouragement. This can help them cope with the challenges of being not able to move or perform certain activities due to a disability.
2. Practical Assistance: Family members can provide practical assistance such as helping with daily tasks, managing schedules, and providing transportation. This can help reduce stress and improve the person's overall quality of life.
3. Advocacy: Family members can act as advocates for their loved one by speaking with healthcare providers, educators, and other service providers to ensure that their needs are being met. They can also advocate for accommodations and support in school, work, or social situations.
4. Financial Support: Family members may provide financial support by paying for medical expenses, assistive devices, and other necessities. This can help reduce financial stress and improve the person's overall well-being.
5. Social Connection: Family members can help their loved one maintain social connections by accompanying them to appointments, social events, and activities. This can help prevent social isolation and improve mental health.
6. Role Modeling: Family members can model positive behaviors and attitudes towards disability, such as acceptance, empathy, and inclusivity. This can help the person with a disability feel more comfortable and confident in their abilities.
7. Building Resilience: Family support can help build resilience by teaching coping skills, problem-solving strategies, and how to manage stress. This can help the person with a disability navigate challenges and setbacks more effectively.
8. Providing a Sense of Belonging: Family support can provide a sense of belonging and connection to others, which is essential for overall well-being. This can help the person with a disability feel more included and valued in society.
