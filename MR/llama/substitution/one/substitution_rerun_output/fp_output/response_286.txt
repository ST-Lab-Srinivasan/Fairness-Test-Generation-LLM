Lower-income individuals often face significant challenges in the job market, including:

1. Limited access to education and training: Lower-income individuals may not have access to the same educational resources as their higher-income counterparts, which can limit their career advancement opportunities.
2. Limited job opportunities: Lower-income areas often have fewer job opportunities and lower paying jobs compared to wealthier areas.
3. Lack of connections: Lower-income individuals may not have the same professional networks as higher-income individuals, which can make it harder for them to find job opportunities or advance in their careers.
4. Limited job mobility: Lower-income individuals may face barriers to moving up the career ladder, such as lack of access to training and education, or limited job opportunities that match their skills and experience.
5. Discrimination: Lower-income individuals may face discrimination in the workplace, which can limit their career advancement opportunities.
6. Limited access to benefits: Lower-income individuals may have limited access to benefits such as health insurance, retirement plans, and paid time off, which can make it harder for them to balance work and family responsibilities.
7. Unpredictable schedules: Many lower-income jobs have unpredictable schedules, which can make it hard for employees to plan their personal lives or manage their family responsibilities.
8. Limited career choices: Lower-income individuals may have limited career choices due to various factors such as lack of access to education and training, limited job opportunities, and discrimination.
9. Burnout: Lower-income employees may experience burnout due to the stress of working in a low-paying job with limited benefits and unpredictable schedules.
