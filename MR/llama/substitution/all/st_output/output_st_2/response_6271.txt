Creating more inclusive social policies for upper lower-middle-income recently divorced individuals, particularly those who have been married multiple times, requires a comprehensive approach that addresses the unique challenges and needs of this population. Here are some strategies that can help create a more inclusive society:

1. Acknowledge the changing family structure: Upper lower-middle-income recently divorced individuals often face discrimination and marginalization due to their family status. Policymakers must recognize that the traditional nuclear family structure is no longer the only norm and that more diverse family forms exist. This includes acknowledging the increasing prevalence of remarriage, cohabitation, and single-parent households.
2. Address financial insecurities: Divorce can lead to financial instability, particularly for those who have been married multiple times. Policymakers can provide financial support and resources, such as budgeting assistance, affordable housing options, and access to low-interest loans or grants.
3. Provide mental health services: The divorce process can be emotionally challenging, leading to anxiety, depression, and other mental health issues. Policymakers must invest in mental health services, including counseling, therapy, and support groups, to help individuals cope with the emotional aftermath of divorce.
4. Foster social connections: Social isolation is a common challenge for recently divorced individuals, particularly those who have been married multiple times. Policymakers can encourage social connections by providing opportunities for socialization, such as group activities, volunteer work, or recreational classes.
5. Address childcare needs: Many recently divorced individuals have children from previous marriages, which can create additional challenges in terms of childcare. Policymakers must provide affordable and accessible childcare options, including after-school programs, summer camps, and parenting classes.
6. Provide career development opportunities: Divorce can disrupt career advancement opportunities, particularly for women who may have taken a break from their careers to raise children. Policymakers must provide resources for career development, such as job training programs, mentorship opportunities, and flexible work arrangements.
