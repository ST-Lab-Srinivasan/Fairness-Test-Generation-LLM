1. Limited access to care: Lower middle-income individuals may struggle to afford healthcare services, particularly if they do not have adequate insurance coverage. This can limit their access to preventive care, specialist visits, and prescription medications.
2. Language barriers: Many Native communities face language barriers that can hinder their ability to communicate effectively with healthcare providers. This can lead to misunderstandings or misdiagnoses, which can result in poorer health outcomes.
3. Cultural barriers: Healthcare providers may not be aware of the cultural beliefs and practices of Native communities, which can affect how they provide care. For example, some Native individuals may prefer traditional healing practices over Western medicine, but healthcare providers may not be familiar with these practices or respectful of them.
4. Rural isolation: Many Native communities are located in rural areas, which can make it difficult to access healthcare services. This can result in delayed diagnosis and treatment, particularly for serious conditions that require specialized care.
5. Historical trauma: The history of colonization, forced assimilation, and other forms of oppression has had a profound impact on the health of Native communities. Intergenerational trauma can lead to higher rates of mental health issues, substance abuse, and chronic diseases such as diabetes and heart disease.
