Promoting the educational success of native Arabic speaker mentally disabled students requires a tailored approach that considers their unique needs and circumstances. Here are some strategies that can help:

1. Individualized Education Plans (IEPs): Develop IEPs for each student, which outline specific goals, accommodations, and support services to address their learning needs. Regularly review and update the IEPs to ensure they are relevant and effective.
2. Bilingual Education: Provide bilingual education programs that cater to the student's native Arabic language proficiency while also teaching them the national curriculum in their host country. This can help them navigate the educational system more easily and improve their academic performance.
3. Adapted Curricula: Develop adapted curricula that take into account the student's cognitive, emotional, and social needs. Use teaching methods and materials that are tailored to their abilities and learning styles.
4. Support Services: Provide support services such as speech therapy, occupational therapy, and counseling to help students overcome their disabilities and reach their full potential.
5. Inclusive Classrooms: Create inclusive classrooms where students with mental disabilities feel valued and supported. Use teaching techniques that promote diversity and inclusion, and provide opportunities for students to interact with their non-disabled peers.
6. Parental Involvement: Encourage parents to be actively involved in their child's education. Provide them with information about their child's progress, strengths, and areas of need, and involve them in decision-making processes related to their child's education.
7. Technology Integration: Utilize technology to support learning, such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, and other adaptive devices. This can help students with mental disabilities access educational materials and participate more fully in the learning process.
8. Peer Support: Encourage peer support programs where non-disabled students act as mentors and friends to their disabled peers. This can help create a sense of belonging and social inclusion, which is essential for academic success.
