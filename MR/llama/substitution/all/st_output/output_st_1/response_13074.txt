Family support plays a crucial role in the success of late teen (18-19) physically disabled people. Here are some ways in which family support can help:

1. Emotional Support: Having a supportive family can provide emotional strength and resilience to deal with the challenges of disability. Family members can offer emotional support, encouragement, and validation, which is essential for mental well-being.
2. Practical Assistance: Family members can assist with daily tasks such as personal care, managing medications, and performing household chores. They can also provide transportation to appointments, social events, and other activities.
3. Advocacy: Family members can act as advocates for the physically disabled individual, helping them navigate the healthcare system, access resources and services, and fight for their rights.
4. Social Inclusion: A supportive family can help the physically disabled individual maintain social connections and avoid social isolation. They can facilitate interactions with other family members, friends, and community groups.
5. Building Self-Esteem: Family support can help build self-esteem and confidence in the face of disability. By providing a nurturing environment, family members can help the individual develop a positive sense of self-worth.
6. Financial Support: In many cases, families may need to provide financial support to cover the costs of disability-related expenses such as medical equipment, therapies, and home modifications.
7. Educational Support: Families can help their teenage member access educational resources and accommodations, ensuring they receive a quality education despite their disability.
8. Career Development: A supportive family can encourage the physically disabled individual to pursue their career goals, providing assistance with job search, resume building, and interview preparation.
9. Building Independence: By providing a nurturing environment, families can help the physically disabled individual develop independence skills, enabling them to live independently as much as possible.
10. Role Modeling: Family members can serve as role models by demonstrating positive attitudes towards disability, challenging societal stereotypes and stigmas, and promoting inclusivity and accessibility.
