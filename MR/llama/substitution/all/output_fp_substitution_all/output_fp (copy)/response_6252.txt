2. Culturally responsive teaching: Recognize that students' cultural backgrounds play a significant role in their learning experiences. Teachers can incorporate culturally relevant materials and practices into the classroom to make students feel seen and included.

4. Family-school partnerships: Encourage families to participate in school events and activities, which can help build relationships between families and schools. This can also help students feel more connected to their school community.
5. Flexibility in academic expectations: Recognize that recently divorced students may be dealing with additional stressors outside of school. Teachers can offer flexibility in academic expectations, such as extensions on deadlines or modified assignments, to help students manage their workload.
6. Diverse teaching methods: Use diverse teaching methods to reach students who may have different learning styles and preferences. This can include visual, auditory, and kinesthetic approaches to teaching and learning.
7. Access to counseling services: Provide access to counseling services for students who may be struggling with the emotional challenges of divorce. School counselors can offer individual and group counseling sessions to help students manage their emotions and develop coping strategies.
