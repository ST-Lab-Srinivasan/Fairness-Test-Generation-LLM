To address this, educators can use culturally responsive teaching practices that incorporate the cultural knowledge, experiences, and perspectives of African-American students. 2. Student-centered learning: Ultra-independent students tend to be self-motivated and autonomous, so it's important to provide them with opportunities for self-directed learning. This can involve giving them choice and autonomy in their learning, allowing them to take ownership of their educational experiences.
3. Personalized learning: Every student is unique, and ultra-independent students are no exception. To promote their educational success, educators can use personalized learning strategies that cater to the individual needs and interests of each student. This can involve tailoring the curriculum to the student's strengths, weaknesses, and learning style.

To address this, educators can prioritize building positive relationships with these students through active listening, empathy, and mutual respect.
5. Addressing systemic barriers: Ultra-independent students may face systemic barriers to success, such as inadequate resources or discrimination. To address these issues, educators can work to dismantle oppressive systems and structures that hinder the educational success of ethnically full African-American students.
