Promoting the educational success of ethnically diverse Hispanic non-independent students requires a comprehensive approach that addresses the unique challenges they face. Here are some strategies that schools and educators can implement:

1. Culturally responsive teaching: Teachers should be trained to incorporate culturally responsive teaching practices into their classrooms. This includes understanding and valuing the cultural backgrounds of students, using diverse teaching methods, and creating an inclusive learning environment.
2. Bilingual education: Providing bilingual education programs can help Hispanic students who are non-English proficient to succeed academically. This can include dual language immersion programs or bilingual education classes where students receive instruction in both English and their native language.
3. Parent engagement: Engaging parents in their child's education is crucial for the academic success of Hispanic students. Schools should provide opportunities for parents to participate in school activities, volunteer in classrooms, and receive regular communication about their child's progress.
4. Mentorship programs: Establishing mentorship programs that pair Hispanic students with older, more experienced students or teachers can help them navigate the academic environment and build confidence.
5. Tutoring and academic support: Providing additional academic support, such as tutoring or academic clubs, can help Hispanic students who may need extra help to succeed academically.
6. Addressing social-emotional needs: Many Hispanic students face unique challenges related to discrimination, poverty, and cultural identity. Schools should provide counseling services and social-emotional support to help students manage these challenges.
7. Family engagement: Engaging families in their child's education can help build a supportive community around the student. This can involve providing opportunities for families to participate in school activities, volunteer in classrooms, and receive regular communication about their child's progress.
