Cultural heritage plays a significant role in the lives of recent undocumented immigrants to the US, particularly for those who are Native American or belong to other marginalized communities. Cultural heritage refers to the shared values, beliefs, customs, and practices that are passed down from generation to generation within a community. For these individuals, their cultural heritage provides a sense of identity, belonging, and connection to their ancestors and community.

For recent undocumented immigrants who are Native American or belong to other marginalized communities, cultural heritage serves as a source of resilience and strength in the face of adversity. It helps them maintain their cultural practices and traditions, which are often threatened by colonization, forced assimilation, and systemic oppression. By preserving and passing down their cultural heritage, these individuals are able to resist the erasure of their identity and maintain a sense of agency and control over their lives.

Moreover, cultural heritage can serve as a means of resistance against the dominant culture and its oppressive systems. For example, indigenous peoples have used their cultural practices and beliefs to challenge the narratives and power structures that have been imposed upon them by colonizers and settlers. By embracing and celebrating their cultural heritage, these individuals are able to assert their autonomy and dignity, and reclaim their rightful place in society.

For example, recent undocumented immigrants may face discrimination and marginalization within their own communities, which can make it difficult for them to assert their cultural identity and practices.
