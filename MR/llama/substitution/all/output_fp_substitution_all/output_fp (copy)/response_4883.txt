Creating more inclusive public spaces for moderate native English speakers requires careful consideration of their needs and preferences. Here are some strategies that can help:

1. Accessibility: Ensure that the public space is accessible to people with disabilities by providing ramps, elevators, and accessible restrooms. This will allow individuals with mobility impairments to fully participate in the space.
2. Acoustic Design: Consider the acoustics of the space and how they may affect individuals with hearing impairments. Use sound-absorbing materials, reduce background noise, and provide assistive listening devices when necessary.
3. Lighting: Provide adequate lighting that is comfortable for all individuals. Avoid harsh or dim lighting that may cause eye strain or discomfort for some users.
4. Wayfinding: Create clear signage and wayfinding systems to help individuals navigate the space easily. This can include Braille signage, large print, or tactile markers to assist individuals with visual impairments.
5. Seating: Provide a variety of seating options that are comfortable for all users. Include seating with backrests, armrests, and footrests to accommodate different preferences and needs.
6. Privacy: Ensure that there are adequate privacy areas within the space for individuals who may need them, such as nursing mothers or individuals who prefer a quiet area to work.
7. Technology: Provide technology that is accessible to all users, such as assistive technologies for individuals with disabilities or mobile devices for individuals who are deaf or hard of hearing.
8. Sensory Integration: Incorporate sensory integration elements into the space, such as texture-rich materials, natural lighting, and soothing colors to create a comfortable environment for all users.
