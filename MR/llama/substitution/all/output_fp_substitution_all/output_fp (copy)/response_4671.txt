As a recently arrived refugee in the United States on a non-immigrant visa, you may face several challenges when accessing healthcare services. Here are some of the common obstacles:

1. Language barriers: Refugees often have limited English proficiency, which can make it difficult to communicate with healthcare providers and navigate the healthcare system.
2. Lack of knowledge about US healthcare system: Refugees may be unfamiliar with the US healthcare system, including its structure, costs, and coverage options.
3. Limited access to health insurance: Non-immigrant visa holders are not eligible for Medicaid or other government-funded health insurance programs. You may need to purchase private insurance, which can be expensive and unaffordable for some individuals.
4. Cost-related barriers: Even if you have health insurance, the out-of-pocket costs of healthcare services can be prohibitively expensive, especially for those with limited financial resources.
5. Cultural and social barriers: Refugees may face cultural and social barriers when seeking healthcare services in the US. For example, they may feel uncomfortable discussing personal health issues with healthcare providers who are from different cultural backgrounds.
6. Fear of deportation: Refugees may be reluctant to seek medical care due to fear of being reported to immigration authorities and facing deportation.
7. Difficulty finding a provider who speaks your language: Many refugees may not be able to find healthcare providers who speak their native language, which can make it difficult to communicate and receive appropriate care.
8. Limited access to specialized care: Refugees may have difficulty accessing specialized medical care, such as mental health services or pediatric care, due to lack of availability or affordability in their area.
9. Lack of transportation: Refugees may face barriers in accessing healthcare services due to lack of transportation or mobility issues.
10. Stigma and discrimination: Refugees may experience stigma and discrimination when seeking medical care, which can further exacerbate their health disparities.
