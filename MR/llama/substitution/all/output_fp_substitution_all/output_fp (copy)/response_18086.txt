Mentorship plays a significant role in the success of non-independent, non-middle-class professional individuals. These individuals often face unique challenges and barriers in their career advancement due to factors such as limited access to resources, networking opportunities, and systemic inequality. Mentorship can help bridge these gaps and provide valuable guidance, support, and connections that are critical for success. Here are some ways mentorship can contribute to the success of non-independent professionals:

1. Networking opportunities: Non-independent professionals may not have the same access to networking opportunities as their more privileged peers. Mentors can provide valuable introductions, recommendations, and connections that can help these individuals expand their professional networks and advance their careers.
2. Skill development: Mentors can identify areas where mentees need improvement and provide targeted guidance and support to help them build the skills they need to succeed. This may include training on specific tools, techniques, or industry-specific knowledge.
3. Personalized advice: Non-independent professionals may face unique challenges that are not addressed in traditional career development programs. Mentors can provide personalized advice and guidance tailored to their mentee's specific circumstances, helping them navigate these challenges more effectively.
4. Confidence building: Mentorship can help non-independent professionals build confidence in their abilities and increase their sense of self-worth. This is particularly important for individuals who may feel marginalized or undervalued due to their socio-economic status.
5. Role modeling: Mentors can serve as role models, demonstrating what it means to be a successful professional in their field. By observing and learning from mentors, mentees can develop a deeper understanding of the values, attitudes, and behaviors that contribute to success.
6. Advocacy: In some cases, mentors may advocate on behalf of their mentees, helping them secure opportunities or overcome obstacles that might not be available or accessible otherwise.
