Supporting the mental health of Native American recent refugees to the US, particularly those who identify as female, requires a comprehensive and culturally sensitive approach. Here are some ways to support their mental health:

1. Culturally responsive services: Provide mental health services that are culturally responsive and tailored to the unique needs of Native American refugees. This includes understanding their cultural beliefs, values, and practices, as well as being sensitive to their experiences of trauma and resilience.
2. Access to traditional healing practices: Many Native American communities have their own traditional healing practices that are rooted in their cultural beliefs and values. Ensure that these practices are accessible and respected, and that they are integrated into mental health services where appropriate.
3. Community-based services: Involve the community in providing mental health services by incorporating traditional healers, elders, and other community members into the treatment process. This can help build trust and cultural relevance.
4. Trauma-informed care: Acknowledge the historical trauma that Native American communities have experienced and ensure that mental health services are trauma-informed. This includes understanding the impact of intergenerational trauma and providing appropriate interventions to address it.
5. Gender-sensitive services: Recognize the unique needs of female-identifying individuals within Native American communities, including their experiences of gender-based violence and discrimination. Provide gender-sensitive services that prioritize their well-being and autonomy.
6. Advocacy and policy changes: Work towards advocating for policy changes that address the systemic barriers to mental health care faced by Native American communities, such as lack of access to healthcare, discrimination, and marginalization.
7. Cultural mentorship: Pair recent refugees with cultural mentors who can provide guidance on how to navigate the mental healthcare system in the US, while also maintaining their cultural identity.
8. Language support: Provide language support services for those who may not speak English fluently, such as interpreters or translated materials.
9. Traditional foods and activities: Incorporate traditional foods and activities into mental health services to help individuals connect with their cultural heritage and find comfort in familiar practices.
