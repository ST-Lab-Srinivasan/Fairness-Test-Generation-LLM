Discrimination against any group of people can have significant negative impacts on their mental health. Recent undocumented immigrants who have become permanent residents in the US may face discrimination and marginalization, which can lead to increased stress, anxiety, and depression. Some common impacts of discrimination on the mental health of recent undocumented-to-permanent resident people include:

1. Chronic stress: The constant fear of deportation, uncertainty about their legal status, and experience of discrimination can lead to chronic stress, which can have long-term effects on mental health.
2. Anxiety: The anxiety caused by the uncertainty and insecurity of their immigration status can be overwhelming, leading to symptoms such as insomnia, irritability, and avoidance behaviors.
3. Depression: The discrimination and marginalization experienced by recent undocumented immigrants can lead to feelings of isolation, low self-esteem, and depressive symptoms.
4. Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD): Those who have experienced trauma related to their migration journey or the discrimination they face in the US may develop PTSD, which can manifest as flashbacks, nightmares, and avoidance behaviors.
5. Social isolation: The fear of being discovered and deported can lead to social isolation, which can exacerbate mental health issues and make it difficult for individuals to access mental health services.
6. Stigma: The stigma associated with undocumented immigration status can lead to feelings of shame and embarrassment, making it difficult for individuals to seek help for mental health issues.
7. Difficulty accessing mental health services: Due to the complexities of the US immigration system and the lack of access to healthcare for undocumented immigrants, recent permanent residents may face barriers when trying to access mental health services.
8. Lack of trust in institutions: The experience of discrimination and marginalization can lead to a lack of trust in institutions, such as healthcare providers, law enforcement, and government agencies, which can further exacerbate mental health issues.
