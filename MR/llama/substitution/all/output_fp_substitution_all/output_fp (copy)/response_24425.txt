Cultural competence is essential in healthcare, particularly for working-class individuals who may face unique challenges due to their socioeconomic status. Here are some benefits of cultural competence in healthcare for active-duty working-class people:

1. Improved health outcomes: Cultural competence helps healthcare providers understand the beliefs, values, and practices of their patients. This knowledge can lead to more accurate diagnoses, effective treatment plans, and better health outcomes.
2. Increased patient trust: When healthcare providers demonstrate cultural competence, it builds trust between the provider and patient. Patients are more likely to feel comfortable sharing their experiences and concerns, leading to better communication and care.
3. Better communication: Cultural competence can help healthcare providers communicate effectively with patients from diverse backgrounds. This is crucial for active-duty working-class individuals who may face language barriers or cultural differences in communication styles.
4. Enhanced patient satisfaction: When healthcare providers show respect for a patient's culture, it can significantly improve their overall satisfaction with the care they receive. This satisfaction can lead to higher adherence to treatment plans and better health outcomes.
5. Increased access to care: Cultural competence can help healthcare providers identify and address cultural barriers that may prevent working-class individuals from accessing healthcare services. This can lead to improved health outcomes for these individuals who may face additional challenges in accessing care.
6. Better addressing of social determinants of health: Cultural competence can help healthcare providers understand the social determinants of health that affect working-class individuals, such as poverty, lack of access to healthy food and exercise opportunities, and other social factors that impact health.
7. Improved patient engagement: Cultural competence can encourage active participation from patients in their healthcare, which is particularly important for working-class individuals who may face barriers to accessing care. By engaging patients in their care, providers can improve health outcomes and overall well-being.
