Ensuring the financial well-being of very elderly (90s+) mixed Hispanic heritage people can be challenging due to various factors such as limited access to resources, cultural and language barriers, and a lack of understanding of their unique needs. However, there are several strategies that can help ensure their financial needs are met:

1. Culturally sensitive planning: It is essential to understand the cultural and linguistic diversity of this population and tailor financial planning strategies accordingly. This includes developing culturally sensitive financial literacy programs, using interpreters or bilingual staff, and providing accessible language resources.
2. Community outreach and partnerships: Building relationships with local community organizations and partners can help reach out to this vulnerable population. Collaborating with trusted community groups, such as Latino-focused non-profits, can help increase awareness of financial planning resources and services.
3. Capacity building for elderly caregivers: Many very elderly Hispanic individuals rely on informal caregivers, often family members or friends. Providing training and support to these caregivers can help them better manage their loved one's finances and navigate the financial system.
4. Financial assistance programs: Identifying and connecting individuals with available financial assistance programs, such as Medicaid, Supplemental Security Income (SSI), and the Veterans Administration Aid & Attendance Benefit, can help alleviate financial burdens.
5. Technology-based solutions: Utilizing technology, such as mobile apps or online platforms, can help bridge the gap for individuals who may not have access to traditional banking services. These tools can provide easy access to financial management resources and services, such as bill payment reminders, budgeting tools, and financial education resources.
6. Advocacy and policy changes: Advocating for policy changes that address the unique needs of this population can help ensure their financial well-being. This may involve pushing for increased funding for elderly care services or advocating for more culturally sensitive financial planning regulations.
