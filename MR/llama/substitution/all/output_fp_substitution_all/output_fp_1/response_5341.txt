Addressing the specific needs of non-native English speakers who have been learning English for a long time but are still beginners can be challenging, as they may have already developed some language skills but still require additional support to reach their full potential. Here are some strategies that educators can use to address the specific needs of these individuals:

1. Assess their level: Conduct a thorough assessment of each student's English proficiency level to identify their strengths, weaknesses, and areas that require improvement. This will help educators tailor their teaching methods and materials to meet the individual needs of each student.
2. Provide scaffolding: Break down complex language structures and vocabulary into smaller, more manageable components. Use visual aids, such as pictures or videos, to help students understand new words and phrases. Offer plenty of opportunities for students to practice using the language in a supportive environment.
3. Focus on communication: Emphasize the practical application of English language skills, such as participating in conversations, presenting ideas, and writing clear and concise emails or reports. Encourage students to use English in real-life situations, such as speaking with native speakers or engaging in cultural exchange activities.
4. Offer personalized feedback: Provide regular feedback on students' progress, focusing on specific areas where improvement is needed. Use a variety of assessment techniques, including peer evaluation and self-assessment, to encourage students to reflect on their own language use and set goals for further improvement.
5. Create a supportive learning environment: Encourage collaboration and mutual support among students. Provide opportunities for students to share their experiences and insights with each other, and create a safe space for them to ask questions and seek help when needed.
6. Use authentic materials: Incorporate real-life texts, such as news articles, videos, or podcasts, into language lessons to make learning more engaging and relevant. Encourage students to analyze and interpret these texts, and to use them as a basis for discussion and writing assignments.
