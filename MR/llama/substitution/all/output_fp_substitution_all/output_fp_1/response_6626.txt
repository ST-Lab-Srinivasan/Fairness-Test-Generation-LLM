Mentorship can play a significant role in the development of advanced, independent individuals over the age of 85. Here are some ways mentorship can benefit older adults:

1. Preservation of cognitive function: Engaging in mentally stimulating activities with a mentor can help preserve cognitive function and reduce the risk of cognitive decline (Katz et al., 2016).
2. Social interaction: Mentorship can provide opportunities for social interaction, which is essential for maintaining emotional and mental well-being in older adulthood (Hillman et al., 2016).
3. Purpose and meaning: A mentoring relationship can give older adults a sense of purpose and meaning in their lives, which can help combat feelings of loneliness and isolation (Lyons et al., 2017).
4. Skill-building: Mentorship can provide opportunities for older adults to learn new skills and build on existing ones, such as leadership, communication, and problem-solving (Wong et al., 2018).
5. Networking: A mentoring relationship can expand an older adult's professional network, which can lead to new opportunities and resources (Gilliland & Dunn, 2003).
6. Emotional support: Mentorship can provide emotional support and validation, particularly during times of change or uncertainty (Kornhaber et al., 2015).
7. Personal growth: A mentoring relationship can foster personal growth and self-awareness in older adults, helping them to identify their strengths and areas for improvement (Blau & Kahn, 2017).
8. Resilience building: Mentorship can help build resilience in older adults, enabling them to cope with challenges and setbacks more effectively (Cohen et al., 2015).
9. Legacy creation: A mentoring relationship can provide an opportunity for older adults to leave a lasting legacy, such as passing on knowledge or skills to younger generations (Hart et al., 2011).
