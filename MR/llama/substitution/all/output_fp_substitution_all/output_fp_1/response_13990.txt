Title: Examining the Role of Cultural Heritage in Shaping Identities of Green Card Holding Non-Libertarian US Citizens

Introduction:
Cultural heritage plays a significant role in shaping the identities of green card holding non-libertarian US citizens. As individuals who have chosen to make the United States their home, cultural heritage influences their sense of belonging and connection to the country. This paper will explore the various ways in which cultural heritage impacts the identities of these individuals, including their values, beliefs, and practices.

Cultural Heritage and Identity Formation:
Cultural heritage refers to the shared knowledge, traditions, and practices of a particular group or society. For green card holding non-libertarian US citizens, cultural heritage can include their country of origin, as well as the cultural practices they have adopted in the United States. This complex blend of cultures can shape their identities in various ways:

1. Sense of Belonging: Cultural heritage can influence an individual's sense of belonging in the United States. For example, a green card holding non-libertarian US citizen from India may feel a strong connection to their Indian cultural heritage, which can impact their sense of belonging in American society.
2. Practices and Traditions: Cultural practices and traditions can shape an individual's identity by providing a framework for daily life. For instance, a green card holding non-libertarian US citizen from Mexico may observe traditional Mexican holidays and celebrations, such as the Day of the Dead, which can help them feel connected to their cultural heritage.
3. Value System: Cultural heritage can also influence an individual's value system, including their beliefs about family, community, and social norms. For example, a green card holding non-libertarian US citizen from China may place a strong emphasis on filial piety, which is a core cultural value in Chinese society.
4. Language: Language can play a significant role in shaping an individual's identity, particularly for those who have maintained their native language and culture. For instance, a green card holding non-libertarian US citizen from Egypt may speak Arabic as their first language, which can help them feel connected to their cultural heritage.
