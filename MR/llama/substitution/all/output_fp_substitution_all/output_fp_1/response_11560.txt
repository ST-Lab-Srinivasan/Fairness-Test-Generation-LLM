The impact of immigration status on the lives of green card holders, also known as lawful permanent residents (LPRs), can be significant and far-reaching. Green card holders are non-citizens with a permanent residence in the United States, but they do not have the same rights and privileges as U.S. citizens. 1. Employment: Green card holders can work in the United States without any restrictions, but they cannot vote or run for public office. They may face challenges when it comes to advancing in their careers or starting their own businesses due to their immigration status.
2. Access to Healthcare: Green card holders are eligible for Medicaid and the Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP), but they may face barriers when it comes to accessing other forms of healthcare, such as subsidized insurance through the Affordable Care Act (ACA).
3. Education: Green card holders are eligible for federal student aid and can attend public universities in the United States at in-state tuition rates, but they may face challenges when it comes to accessing certain scholarships or financial aid programs.
4. Travel: Green card holders must have a valid passport and an Arrival/Departure Record (I-94) document to travel outside of the United States, and they may face delays or denials at ports of entry if they are found to be in violation of U.S. immigration laws.
5. Social Security Benefits: Green card holders are not eligible for Social Security benefits, including retirement, disability, and survivor benefits.
6. Voting Rights: Green card holders are not eligible to vote in federal elections, although some states allow non-citizens to vote in local elections.
