Promoting the educational success of mixed African American students can be challenging, especially when they exhibit extreme independence. Here are some strategies that educators and parents can use to support these students:

1. Embrace diversity: Create an inclusive learning environment that values and embraces diversity. Encourage students to share their experiences and perspectives, and provide opportunities for them to connect with peers from different backgrounds.
2. Foster a growth mindset: Teach students that intelligence can be developed and that they have the power to achieve their goals through effort and learning. Provide opportunities for students to take on challenges and reflect on their progress.
3. Encourage self-directed learning: Extreme independence can be both an asset and a challenge. Encourage students to take ownership of their learning by setting goals, creating learning plans, and tracking their progress.
4. Provide opportunities for autonomy: Allow students to make choices about their learning, such as selecting topics or methods of expression. This can help them feel more invested in their education and more likely to succeed.
5. Offer flexible pacing: Some students may need more time to master certain skills or concepts, while others may be ready for more advanced material. Provide opportunities for students to work at their own pace and choose the path that best meets their needs.
6. Provide individualized support: Each student is unique, and they may have different strengths, weaknesses, and learning styles. Offer individualized support and resources to help them succeed.
7. Encourage self-advocacy: Teach students how to advocate for themselves, including how to communicate their needs and goals to teachers and peers. This can help them become more confident and independent learners.
8. Provide opportunities for real-world applications: Help students see the relevance of what they're learning in school to their lives outside of school. Provide opportunities for them to apply what they've learned to real-world situations.
