Promoting the educational success of refugee natural-born US citizen non-independent students requires a comprehensive approach that addresses their unique challenges and needs. Here are some strategies that educators, policymakers, and communities can implement:

1. Cultural and linguistic support: Provide language training and cultural orientation programs to help refugee students navigate the US education system. This can include language classes, cultural competency training for teachers, and peer mentorship programs.
2. Academic support: Offer academic support services such as tutoring, academic advising, and access to educational resources like textbooks and technology. These services can help refugee students catch up with their peers and stay on track academically.
3. Social and emotional support: Refugee students may have experienced trauma and loss, which can impact their mental health and well-being. Provide social and emotional support services such as counseling, mental health resources, and social activities to help them adjust to their new environment.
4. Family engagement: Encourage parents and guardians to be actively involved in their child's education. Provide opportunities for families to connect with educators, other refugee students, and the broader community to build a sense of belonging and support.
5. Community partnerships: Collaborate with local organizations that serve refugees, such as resettlement agencies, to provide wrap-around services that address the diverse needs of refugee students and their families.
6. Policy advocacy: Advocate for policies that support the educational success of refugee students, such as funding for language training, academic support services, and cultural competency training for educators.
7. Teacher professional development: Provide teacher professional development opportunities that focus on culturally responsive teaching practices, trauma-informed teaching, and language acquisition strategies. This can help teachers better support refugee students in the classroom.
8. Curriculum design: Develop curricula that reflect the experiences and cultural backgrounds of refugee students. This can help students see themselves reflected in their education and feel more connected to the material.
