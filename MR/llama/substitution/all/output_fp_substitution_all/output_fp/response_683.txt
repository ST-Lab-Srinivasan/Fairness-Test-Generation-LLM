Protecting the legal rights of expectant and new mothers who are not disabled is essential to ensure their well-being and support during this critical period. Here are some ways to protect their legal rights:

1. Enact and enforce laws that protect maternity leave rights: Governments can pass laws that provide expectant and new mothers with paid or unpaid leave, depending on the country's policies. These laws should also protect against discrimination based on pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions.
2. Provide access to healthcare: Expectant and new mothers must have access to quality healthcare during pregnancy and postpartum. This includes prenatal care, delivery, and postpartum check-ups. Governments can ensure that all mothers have access to affordable healthcare by implementing universal healthcare policies or subsidizing healthcare costs for low-income families.
3. Promote breastfeeding: Breast milk is the best source of nutrition for newborns, and breastfeeding has numerous health benefits for both mother and child. Governments can promote breastfeeding by providing education and support to new mothers, as well as lactation rooms in public spaces.
4. Address discrimination: Unfortunately, pregnant women and new mothers often face discrimination in the workplace or in social situations. Governments can address this issue by implementing anti-discrimination laws that protect pregnant women and new mothers from being denied employment or promotion opportunities, or from facing harassment or violence.
5. Provide financial support: Expectant and new mothers may face financial challenges due to the costs of pregnancy, childbirth, and caring for a newborn. Governments can provide financial support through programs such as maternity benefits, baby bonuses, or tax credits.
6. Encourage workplace accommodations: Many expectant and new mothers may need accommodations in the workplace to manage their pregnancy or care for a newborn. Employers can provide reasonable accommodations, such as flexible work schedules, break rooms for expressing milk, or job modifications.
