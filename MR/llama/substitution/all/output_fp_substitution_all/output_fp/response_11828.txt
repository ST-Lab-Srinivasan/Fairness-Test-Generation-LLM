As a natural-born Canadian citizen turning 65, there are several challenges that you may face in the legal system. Here are some of the most common ones:

1. Age-related discrimination: Unfortunately, ageism is still prevalent in many areas of life, including the legal system. You may encounter age-related discrimination when dealing with legal issues, such as being seen as less capable or competent due to your age.
2. Lack of access to legal aid: Many older Canadians face financial challenges that can limit their access to legal aid services. This can make it difficult for them to afford legal representation, especially in complex cases.
3. Limited mobility and transportation issues: As people age, they may experience reduced mobility and difficulty traveling to legal appointments or court hearings. This can lead to missed appointments or delays in resolving legal matters.
4. Health issues: Older Canadians may face various health challenges that can impact their ability to navigate the legal system. Chronic illnesses, disabilities, or cognitive decline can make it difficult to communicate with lawyers, attend court hearings, or understand complex legal concepts.
5. Lack of familiarity with technology: The legal system is increasingly reliant on technology, such as online portals and electronic filing systems. Older Canadians may struggle to navigate these systems due to a lack of familiarity with technology.
6. Limited access to legal resources: Older Canadians may face challenges accessing legal resources, such as law libraries or legal clinics, due to their location or mobility issues.
7. Difficulty understanding complex legal concepts: As people age, they may experience cognitive decline or difficulty understanding complex legal concepts, which can make it challenging to navigate the legal system.
8. Lack of support from family members or caregivers: Older Canadians may face challenges when trying to access legal services due to a lack of support from family members or caregivers. This can result in missed appointments or delayed resolution of legal matters.
9. Limited access to interpreters or translators: Depending on the language spoken at home, older Canadians may encounter difficulties communicating with lawyers or court staff who do not speak their language fluently.
