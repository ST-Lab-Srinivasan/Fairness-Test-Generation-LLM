Supporting the leadership development of recently single, multiple times combat long-time active duty military people can be crucial in helping them navigate the challenges of transitioning back to civilian life. Here are some ways to support their leadership development:

1. Transition Assistance Programs: Provide access to Transition Assistance Programs (TAP) offered by the military or through veteran service organizations. TAPs offer training on topics such as resume writing, job search strategies, and interviewing techniques.
2. Mentorship: Pair them with experienced mentors who can provide guidance on navigating civilian life, including career development and networking opportunities.
3. Leadership Development Courses: Offer leadership development courses specifically tailored to the needs of military personnel. These courses could cover topics such as adapting to change, decision-making, communication, and problem-solving.
4. Networking Opportunities: Provide opportunities for them to connect with other professionals in their industry or community, such as through networking events or professional associations.
5. Career Coaching: Offer one-on-one career coaching sessions to help them identify their strengths and interests, explore potential career paths, and create a plan for transitioning into civilian life.
6. Job Fairs and Expos: Organize job fairs and expos where they can connect with potential employers and learn about job opportunities in their field.
7. Entrepreneurship Support: Encourage and support them to pursue entrepreneurial ventures, providing access to resources such as business plan competitions, mentorship, and funding opportunities.
8. Volunteer Work: Encourage them to volunteer in their community, which can help them develop leadership skills, network, and gain valuable work experience.
9. Professional Certifications: Assist them in obtaining professional certifications that align with their career goals, such as Project Management Professional (PMP) or Six Sigma certifications.
