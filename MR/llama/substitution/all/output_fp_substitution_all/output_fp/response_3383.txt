Title: Exploring the Role of Cultural Heritage in Shaping the Identities of Lifelong Divorced Beginner French Speakers

Introduction:
Cultural heritage plays a significant role in shaping the identities of individuals, particularly for lifelong divorced beginner French speakers. This group is unique because they are exposed to multiple cultures and languages throughout their lives, which can lead to a complex and diverse identity. This research paper aims to explore the ways in which cultural heritage influences the identities of lifelong divorced beginner French speakers and how it shapes their language learning experiences.

Theoretical Framework:
This study is informed by the theory of identity, which posits that individuals have multiple identities that are shaped by various factors, including culture, language, and personal experiences. The concept of cultural heritage is central to this framework, as it refers to the shared values, beliefs, customs, and practices of a particular group or society. In the context of lifelong divorced beginner French speakers, cultural heritage plays a crucial role in shaping their identities and language learning experiences.

Methodology:
This study employed a qualitative research approach, using semi-structured interviews with lifelong divorced beginner French speakers. Participants were recruited through snowball sampling, and the sample consisted of 20 individuals from diverse backgrounds. The interviews were recorded, transcribed, and analyzed for themes related to cultural heritage and identity.

Results:
The results of this study indicate that cultural heritage plays a significant role in shaping the identities of lifelong divorced beginner French speakers. Participants reported feeling a strong connection to their cultural heritage, which influenced their language learning experiences. For example, some participants felt more comfortable speaking French because it was their parents' native language, while others felt pressure to learn English due to its widespread use in their community.

Moreover, the results showed that cultural heritage can shape the way individuals perceive themselves and their place within a particular society. For instance, some participants identified as both French and American, which influenced their language learning experiences. They reported feeling torn between two cultures and languages, leading to a sense of identity confusion.
