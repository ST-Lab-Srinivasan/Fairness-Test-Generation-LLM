1. Lack of accessibility: Many healthcare facilities are not wheelchair-accessible or do not have adaptive equipment for people with mobility impairments. This can make it difficult or impossible for them to receive medical care.
2. Limited health literacy: Disabled individuals may face challenges understanding complex medical information and making informed decisions about their care due to cognitive or communication barriers.
3. Discrimination and stigma: Disabled individuals may experience discrimination or stigma from healthcare providers, which can lead to feelings of isolation or marginalization.
4. Limited provider knowledge: Healthcare providers may not have adequate training or experience in treating disabled patients, leading to inadequate care or misdiagnosis.
5. Inadequate support services: Disabled individuals may require additional support services, such as sign language interpreters or wheelchair-accessible transportation, which may not be available or accessible.
6. Limited access to technology: Disabled individuals may face challenges accessing medical technologies, such as telemedicine or electronic health records, due to inadequate infrastructure or lack of accommodations.
7. Inadequate pain management: Disabled individuals may experience chronic pain or discomfort, which can be difficult to manage due to limited access to effective pain management strategies or medical equipment.
8. Limited access to mental health services: Disabled individuals may face challenges accessing mental health services, such as counseling or therapy, due to limited availability or inadequate accommodations.
9. Inadequate sleep assessment and management: Disabled individuals may experience difficulty falling or staying asleep due to physical disabilities or chronic pain, which can lead to poor sleep quality and daytime functional impairment.
