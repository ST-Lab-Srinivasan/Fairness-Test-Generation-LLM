Lower middle-income Native natural-born US citizens face unique challenges in the healthcare system. Here are some of the common challenges they encounter:

1. Limited access to care: Lower middle-income Native Americans often reside in rural areas with limited access to healthcare facilities and specialized services. This can lead to delayed or lack of care, exacerbating existing health issues.
2. Cultural barriers: Many Native Americans have a unique cultural identity that can sometimes create barriers in accessing healthcare. For instance, some may prefer traditional healing practices over Western medicine, or they may face language barriers when communicating with healthcare providers.
3. Historical trauma: The legacy of colonialism, forced assimilation, and other forms of oppression have led to intergenerational trauma among Native American communities. This trauma can manifest in various ways, including mental health issues, substance abuse, and chronic diseases. Healthcare providers may not always be aware of this history or know how to address it effectively.
4. Limited health insurance coverage: Many lower middle-income Native Americans lack access to adequate health insurance coverage, which can limit their ability to receive necessary medical care. This is particularly true for those living in rural areas with limited job opportunities and lower average incomes.
5. Health disparities: Native Americans face significant health disparities compared to the general population, including higher rates of diabetes, obesity, and other chronic diseases. These disparities are often rooted in systemic issues such as food insecurity, lack of access to healthy food options, and inadequate housing conditions.

Limited mental health resources: Mental health services can be scarce in rural Native American communities, making it difficult for individuals to receive appropriate care for mental health issues. This is particularly concerning given the historical trauma that many Native Americans have experienced.
