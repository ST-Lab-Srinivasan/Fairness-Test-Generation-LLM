1. Increasing the number of community health workers, telemedicine services, and mobile health clinics can help address these barriers and improve access to care.
2. Improve cultural competency: Healthcare providers must be trained to understand and address the unique cultural and social determinants of health that affect deep in poverty African American communities. This includes developing culturally sensitive patient education materials, training providers on unconscious bias and cultural humility, and incorporating community-based perspectives into care delivery.
3. Address systemic issues: Deep in poverty African Americans often face systemic barriers to healthcare access, including discrimination, lack of transportation, and limited healthcare provider options. Addressing these issues requires policy changes and advocacy efforts to increase funding for community-based healthcare services and to promote equity and inclusion in healthcare delivery.
4. Promote prevention and early intervention: Many deep in poverty African Americans face barriers to accessing preventive care, including lack of access to healthy food options, safe living conditions, and limited opportunities for physical activity. Promoting prevention and early intervention through community-based programs can help address these barriers and improve overall health outcomes.

Implementing culturally sensitive mental health services that address these challenges can help improve mental health outcomes and overall well-being.
