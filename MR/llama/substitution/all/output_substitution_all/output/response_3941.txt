1. Culturally responsive teaching: Native American students have unique cultural backgrounds and experiences that can enrich their educational experience. Teachers can incorporate traditional teachings, stories, and practices into the classroom to make learning more engaging and relevant. This approach is known as culturally responsive teaching.
2. Trauma-informed instruction: Many Native American students have experienced trauma related to displacement, violence, or cultural suppression. Teachers can use trauma-informed instruction to create a safe and supportive learning environment that acknowledges the students' experiences and promotes healing. This may involve incorporating traditional healing practices into the classroom, such as mindfulness exercises or storytelling.
3. Language preservation: Native American languages are an essential part of their cultural heritage. Teachers can promote language preservation by incorporating Native American languages into the classroom curriculum and encouraging students to use their native languages in academic settings.
4. Community partnerships: Building partnerships with local Native American communities can help connect students with their cultural heritage and provide additional support. Educators can collaborate with tribal elders, community leaders, and cultural practitioners to develop culturally responsive curriculum and programs that promote student success.

Identity-based mentorship: Providing identity-based mentorship opportunities can help Native American students connect with role models and mentors who share their cultural background and experiences.
