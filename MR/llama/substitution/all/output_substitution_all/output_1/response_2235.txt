Ensuring that the healthcare needs of lower-class people in Australia, particularly those who are beginners or non-English speakers, are met requires a multi-faceted approach. Here are some strategies that can help:

1. Culturally sensitive care: Healthcare providers must be aware of the cultural background and values of their patients. This includes being sensitive to language barriers, religious beliefs, and customs. Providers should be trained in culturally competent care and work towards creating a welcoming environment for all patients.
2. Language support: Providing interpretation and translation services can help bridge the language gap between healthcare providers and lower-class non-English speakers. This can include on-site interpreters, telephone interpretation services, or using video remote interpreting (VRI) technology.
3. Outreach programs: Healthcare providers can develop outreach programs to reach lower-class communities, particularly those who may have limited access to healthcare services. These programs can provide education on healthy behaviors, disease prevention, and early detection of illnesses.
4. Community partnerships: Building partnerships with community organizations and social service agencies can help identify and address the healthcare needs of lower-class people. These partnerships can facilitate referrals for healthcare services, as well as provide support for patients who may need additional assistance beyond medical care.
5. Affordable care: Ensuring that healthcare services are affordable and accessible to all, including lower-class individuals, is crucial. This can involve reducing costs through Medicaid expansion, subsidies for private insurance, or implementing a universal healthcare system.

Addressing social determinants of health: Lower-class people may face barriers in accessing healthcare due to social determinants such as poverty, lack of transportation, and limited access to healthy food and housing. Addressing these determinants can help improve overall health outcomes and reduce health disparities.
7. Training for providers: Providers should be trained to recognize and address the unique healthcare needs of lower-class people, including those with complex social and cultural issues.
